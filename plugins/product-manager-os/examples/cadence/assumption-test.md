# Assumption Test: Blocker Detector

**TL;DR**
- Blocker Detector flags at-risk work from written check-ins so managers act before a blocker stalls a sprint. It rests on four leap-of-faith bets — only the desirability and viability ones are truly unevidenced.
- Riskiest bets, in order: (1) managers actually act on a flag, (2) check-in text contains enough signal to detect a real blocker, (3) teams trust the flags enough not to mute them. We can test all three for ~$0 cash and 9 working days using data we already have.
- Kill criterion: if managers act on fewer than 1 in 4 flags in a 3-week concierge test, we stop and reroute the engineering effort to week-2 retention.

**Idea (one sentence):** A digest feature that reads daily written check-ins and flags work that looks at risk of slipping (blocked, stale, or repeatedly carried over), for engineering managers at 20–200 person tech companies.
**Target user:** Engineering managers / team leads running async standups in Cadence — hiring it to catch a stalling task before it costs a sprint, without DMing five people to find out.
**Intended outcome:** Lift week-2 retention and create an upsell wedge. Mechanism: a flag the manager acts on creates a visible "Cadence caught that" moment → manager keeps the team checking in → premium tier later carries the feature.
**Known vs. believed:** *Known* — ~4,000 paying teams; many stop checking in after week 1 (the retention problem we already track); we have ~14 months of check-in text. *Believed* — that managers will act on a flag, that the text carries enough signal to detect real blockers, that teams won't see flags as surveillance.
**Constraints:** Time/budget to validate before building: **2 weeks, ~$2,000 cash ceiling**. Team: 1 PM, 3 engineers (1 on PTO 3 days this sprint), 1 designer, shared data analyst. Technical: no LLM inference in the product pipeline yet.

## For this to succeed, all of these must be true
We assume the words teams type into a check-in actually contain a detectable signal that work is at risk — and that we can separate it from normal "still working on it" noise. We assume that when we surface a flag, the manager does something with it (pings the person, reprioritizes, unblocks) rather than ignoring one more notification. We assume the team being flagged reads it as help, not as the tool tattling on them, so they keep checking in instead of quietly opting out. And we assume the detection is cheap and reliable enough to run on every team's daily check-ins without blowing up cost or drowning managers in false alarms.

## Riskiest assumptions (the leap-of-faith bets)
The top 3 by risk score — test these before building anything:

### RA1 — Check-in text carries enough signal to detect a real blocker (Lens: F · Risk: 20)
- **Hypothesis:** In a sample of 300 real check-ins, at least 60% of work that later slipped showed a detectable textual signal (explicit blocker, repeated carry-over, or hedging language) in the check-in *before* the slip.
- **Cheapest test:** Existing-data spike. Analyst pulls 300 check-ins from teams where we can see whether the referenced task slipped (carried over 2+ days or marked late). PM + analyst hand-label each: did the prior check-in contain a detectable at-risk signal? No model built — this tests whether the signal exists at all before we build a detector for it. Collapses #5 and #11 (false-positive feel and label consistency are observed while labeling).
- **Metric:** Recall of detectable signal among items that actually slipped (% of slips that had a prior textual signal).
- **Baseline or holdout:** Read against the false-positive side — also label 150 check-ins on work that did *not* slip, to get the rate of "signal present but nothing slipped." Recall is meaningless without this; a detector that flags everything scores 100% recall and is useless.
- **Pass / fail threshold (set now):** Pass if recall ≥ 60% AND false-positive rate ≤ 30%; fail if recall < 60% OR false-positive rate > 30%.
- **Cost & time:** $0 cash · 3 days (analyst + PM labeling).
- **If pass →** the signal exists; build a thin keyword+LLM detector for the concierge test (RA2).  ·  **If fail →** the text is too sparse; the feature can't work as scoped — pivot to prompting better blocker entry first, or kill.

### RA2 — Managers will act on a flag rather than ignore it (Lens: D · Risk: 20)
- **Hypothesis:** At least 25% of flags surfaced to managers result in an observable action (ping, reprioritize, unblock, reply) within 24 hours.
- **Cheapest test:** Concierge / Wizard-of-Oz. With 8 recruited teams, PM + analyst manually read daily check-ins and inject a hand-written flag into the manager's digest ("Cadence flagged: API migration looks stalled — 3 days no movement"). No detector shipped — a human plays the model. Run 3 weeks. Track whether the manager acts.
- **Metric:** % of injected flags that produce a manager action within 24h.
- **Baseline or holdout:** 4 of the 8 teams are a holdout that gets the normal digest with no flags — compare slip-resolution rate and manager-initiated pings between flagged and unflagged teams, so we know the flag caused the action rather than managers who'd have acted anyway.
- **Pass / fail threshold (set now):** Pass if ≥ 25% of flags drive an action AND flagged teams resolve slips faster than holdout; fail if action rate < 25%.
- **Cost & time:** ~$500 (recruiting incentive) · 5 days setup + 3 weeks runtime (PM/analyst ~30 min/day).
- **If pass →** managers want and use the flag; spec the detector and a digest slot.  ·  **If fail →** surfacing a flag changes nothing — kill the feature and redirect engineering to week-2 retention directly.

### RA3 — Flagged teams read it as help, not surveillance (Lens: D · Risk: 15)
- **Hypothesis:** Among teams that received flags in the RA2 concierge test, week-2 check-in rate is no lower than the holdout — i.e. flagging does not depress the very behavior we're trying to protect.
- **Cheapest test:** Tested within RA2 — it can fail independently (managers act on flags AND teams quietly stop checking in), so it gets its own measured metric and 6 short exit interviews, not a separate experiment.
- **Metric:** Week-2 check-in completion rate, flagged teams vs. holdout (delta in percentage points).
- **Baseline or holdout:** The same 4-team holdout from RA2.
- **Pass / fail threshold (set now):** Pass if flagged-team week-2 check-in rate is within −3pp of holdout or better; fail if flagged teams drop more than 3pp below holdout (signal that flags feel like surveillance and accelerate churn).
- **Cost & time:** $0 incremental · runs inside RA2's 3 weeks; 6 exit interviews = 1 day.
- **If pass →** flags are safe to ship to whole teams, not just managers.  ·  **If fail →** rework framing (manager-only visibility, opt-in, softer language) before any rollout, or kill if even reframed it depresses check-ins.

## Kill criteria
We stop the Blocker Detector if **either**: (a) RA1 fails — fewer than 60% of real slips had any detectable textual signal, meaning there is nothing reliable to detect; or (b) RA2 fails — managers act on fewer than 1 in 4 flags, meaning the flag changes no behavior. Either result means the engineering effort should reroute to week-2 retention directly rather than via this feature. A surveillance backlash in RA3 pauses (does not necessarily kill) the team-wide rollout.
