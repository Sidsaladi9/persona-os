# personas — worked example (Cadence)

> Generated by the Product Manager OS `personas` skill. Demo company: Cadence.

---

**TL;DR**
- Two personas drive Cadence's roadmap: **Accountability Marcus** (the engineering manager who buys and renews) and **Heads-down Priya** (the IC who has to actually check in). They want opposite things, which is the whole tension.
- Both are evidence-based (40 win/loss + onboarding interviews, Amplitude, support tags); the few gaps are tagged `[ASSUMPTION]` with a validation plan.
- This sharpens our two priorities: week-2 retention is a **Priya** problem (the check-in costs her more than it returns), and Blocker Detector is a **Marcus** feature we must ship without taxing Priya.

## What we have to work with
- 22 onboarding/CS interviews (Q1–Q2 2026) + 18 win/loss call notes (Gong).
- Amplitude: 4,012 paying teams; check-in completion by week; activation funnel.
- Support tags (Intercom): top reasons teams contact us.
- Stripe: plan mix (Team $4/user/mo vs enterprise custom).
- We saw **two** distinct behavior patterns that change what we'd build: the person who reads the digest and the person who writes the check-in. They are not the same user, and conflating them is why "the user" kept meaning two things in roadmap meetings.

### Accountability Marcus — engineering manager who replaced standup with Cadence and lives in the digest

**Goals**
- Know what his team is doing and what's stuck, without sitting in a 15-minute standup that derails focus time across 6 people.
- Catch at-risk work early enough to unblock it before it slips a sprint.
- Justify the tool's cost at renewal: prove it saved meeting time and surfaced problems sooner.

**Jobs-to-be-done**
- When I start my morning, I want to read one digest of who's blocked, so I can spend my 1:1s on the 2 people who actually need me.
- When a piece of work is quietly stalling, I want to be told before standup would have caught it, so I can intervene while it's cheap.
- When renewal comes up, I want evidence the tool earned its seat cost, so I can defend the line item to my VP.

**Pains / frustrations**
- The digest tells him what people *did*, not what's *at risk* — he still finds out about blockers too late (this is the gap Blocker Detector targets).
- When his team stops checking in (common after week 1), the digest goes empty and he can't tell if work is fine or just unreported.
- He bought Cadence to kill standup, but a half-adopted team gives him the worst of both: no meeting *and* no signal.

**Context & triggers**
- 20–200 person tech company; manages 4–8 ICs across 1–2 squads. Often a former senior engineer, recently promoted, protective of focus time.
- Reached for an async tool after a distributed/hybrid shift made a live standup span 3+ time zones.
- Evaluates against Geekbot and "we'll just use a Slack channel"; the deciding factor in win/loss was the digest, not the check-in form.

**Current behavior / workarounds**
- Reads the digest daily; skims, hunts for the one line that signals trouble.
- When check-ins dry up, reverts to DMing people individually ("hey, still on track for Thursday?") — exactly the overhead he was trying to remove.
- Some teams keep a parallel Notion or Linear view for "real" status, treating Cadence as ceremony — a churn warning sign.

**How this changes what we build**
- Prioritize **Blocker Detector** surfaced *inside the digest* (not a separate tab) — Marcus already lives in the digest; meet him there.
- Build a manager-visible "team participation" signal so he can see adoption slipping before it becomes empty digests (ties directly to week-2 retention).
- Do **not** add more configuration knobs to the check-in form to please power users — Marcus values a digest he can trust in 60 seconds, not flexibility.

**Evidence**
- Win/loss: 13 of 18 named the manager digest as the primary reason they chose or stayed with Cadence (Gong, Q1–Q2 2026).
- Support tags: "blocker surfaced too late" / "didn't know work was stuck" = 31% of manager-initiated tickets (Intercom).
- Interviews: 9 of 22 managers described reverting to individual DMs when check-ins dropped off.
- `[ASSUMPTION]` Marcus weighs participation health heavily at renewal — validate by: 6 renewal-cycle interviews + correlate team participation rate against renewal in Stripe/Amplitude.

### Heads-down Priya — IC engineer who has to write the check-in and isn't sure who reads it

**Goals**
- Protect deep-work blocks; spend as little time as possible on status overhead.
- Communicate she's on track (and ask for help when she isn't) without writing a mini-essay every morning.
- Avoid feeling surveilled — a check-in that reads as a productivity report is one she'll quietly abandon.

**Jobs-to-be-done**
- When I sit down to code, I want to clear the check-in in under a minute, so I can get into flow.
- When I'm genuinely blocked, I want a low-friction way to flag it, so I get help without scheduling a meeting.
- When yesterday looks like today, I want to not repeat myself, so the check-in doesn't feel like busywork.

**Pains / frustrations**
- The daily prompt interrupts at the wrong time and asks the same three questions even when nothing changed — it feels like a form, not communication.
- She's not sure the digest is even read, so the check-in feels like effort with no payoff — the core reason she stops by week 2.
- Repetitive, low-signal check-ins ("same as yesterday, still on the API refactor") make her feel like she's performing work rather than doing it.

**Context & triggers**
- IC engineer on Marcus's squad; 2–6 years' experience; treats Slack/Linear as home base and resents context-switching out of them.
- Onboarded by her manager, not by choice — adoption is top-down, so her baseline motivation is low.
- The check-in reminder fires on a schedule (e.g., 9:30am) that often lands mid-focus or before she's started.

**Current behavior / workarounds**
- Week 1: completes check-ins because it's new and the manager is watching. Week 2: starts skipping, or pastes one-line copies of yesterday.
- Eventually checks in only when pinged, or routes status straight to Slack/Linear where her team already talks — bypassing Cadence entirely.
- Treats the prompt as interruptible: dismisses the notification "to do later," and later never comes.

**How this changes what we build**
- Make the check-in **prefill from yesterday + connected tools** (Linear/GitHub) so Priya confirms-and-edits in <30s instead of writing from scratch — directly attacks week-2 drop-off.
- Close the loop: show her a lightweight "your blocker was seen / your manager read this" signal so the effort visibly pays off.
- Let the reminder be **smart-timed / snoozable to a focus-friendly slot** rather than a fixed cron ping; do not punish skips with nags (nagging accelerates the exact abandonment we're fighting).
- Blocker Detector must flag at-risk work **from the check-in she already writes** — it cannot ask Priya to do more, or it dies on arrival.

**Evidence**
- Amplitude: check-in completion drops from 78% (week 1) to 41% (week 2) to 29% (week 4) across teams onboarded Jan–Apr 2026 — the headline week-2 retention problem.
- Interviews: 7 of 12 ICs said they "weren't sure anyone read it"; 5 of 12 described pasting near-identical updates.
- Support/Amplitude: 36% of check-in dismissals happen within 5 minutes of the reminder firing, clustering around 9:00–10:00am (notification-timing signal).
- `[ASSUMPTION]` Prefill from Linear/GitHub raises week-2 completion materially — validate by: prototype prefill flow + A/B test completion rate against the blank-form control over 4 weeks.

### Anti-persona: Process Dana — the ops/PMO lead who wants Cadence as a compliance and reporting system of record

- Dana wants mandatory fields, locked schedules, exportable per-person activity reports, and "who missed their check-in" leaderboards rolled up to leadership.
- Serving Dana means adding required fields, rigid timing, and surveillance-flavored reporting — every one of which raises friction and the *surveilled* feeling for **Heads-down Priya**, accelerating the week-2 drop-off that is our #1 problem.
- We optimize for the manager who wants *signal* (Marcus) and the IC who wants *low friction* (Priya). We explicitly decline the compliance/enforcement use case, even though it's enterprise-tempting, because winning Dana loses Priya — and without Priya checking in, there is no digest for Marcus and nothing for Blocker Detector to read.
