# Red Team Review: Q3 strategy — fix week-2 retention by shipping the Blocker Detector

**Reviewer stance:** Adversarial • **Date:** 2026-06-17 • **Stakes:** ~1.5 engineers for the quarter (one of three on 3-day PTO), the designer at 50%, and the analyst part-time. The bet anchors the Q3 roadmap and the renewal story for ~4,000 teams. Reversible as code, hard to reverse as narrative — we've already told two enterprise prospects this is coming.

> **TL;DR**
> - We're betting that an AI feature (Blocker Detector) fixes a behavior problem (teams stop checking in after week 1). That's a likely root-cause mismatch — teams who churn aren't there to see the feature.
> - Verdict is SPLIT: the retention strategy is 🔴 RETHINK; the Blocker Detector as a manager-value feature is 🟡 SHIP WITH FIXES.
> - Smallest save: separate the two bets, instrument *why* week-2 drop-off happens before building, and gate the launch on a holdout test — not a before/after number.

## The bet, stated plainly
This plan is betting that **shipping the Blocker Detector will lift week-2 retention, because surfacing at-risk work will make check-ins feel valuable enough that teams keep doing them.** If that's true, it works.

## Steelman (the strongest version)
The honest case for this plan is strong. Cadence's whole pitch is "replace the standup with something that gives managers more than a standup did" — and right now the async check-in gives the *individual* a chore and the *manager* a digest, but neither gets a visible "aha." The Blocker Detector is the first feature that turns raw check-in text into a manager action ("Maya's been blocked on the migration for 3 days"). If managers see that value, they enforce the ritual, and team-level habit follows manager pull. Bundling the retention fix with the headline feature is also efficient for a 1-PM team: one bet, one launch, one story for renewals and the two enterprise prospects already in the pipeline. It's a coherent, sellable narrative.

## Verdict
**SPLIT — Strategy: RETHINK • Artifact: SHIP WITH FIXES.**
- **Retention strategy: 🔴 RETHINK** — the plan assumes the cause of week-2 churn (low perceived value) without proving it, and the proposed fix reaches the wrong person (the manager) to fix a contributor-side drop-off.
- **Blocker Detector as a feature: 🟡 SHIP WITH FIXES** — it's a genuine manager-value add and worth building, *if* we stop pretending it's a retention lever and gate the launch on a real test.

> **Assumptions I couldn't verify / inputs I'd need:** the actual week-2 drop-off mechanism (do teams stop *as a team*, or do individuals trail off?); whether churned teams ever reached the digest; current digest open rate by manager; baseline blocker-detection precision on real check-in text. With those four, this becomes a finished call instead of a probabilistic one.

## Load-bearing assumptions
| # | Assumption | If false, then... | Confidence it's true | Load-bearing? |
|---|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| 1 | Teams stop checking in because the ritual feels low-value (not because the manager stopped enforcing it, or onboarding never set the habit) | The Detector solves a problem teams don't have; retention doesn't move | Low | 🔴 Critical |
| 2 | The people who churn in week 2 are *managers* (the ones who see the Detector), or manager pull reliably revives contributor habit | We ship value to the wrong user; the at-risk teams never see it | Low | 🔴 Critical |
| 3 | The Detector is accurate enough on real check-in text that managers trust it | False positives train managers to ignore it; it becomes noise, not value | Med | 🔴 Critical |
| 4 | A team that's already drifting will *open the digest* to encounter the Detector at all | A drifting team has stopped reading; the feature can't reach them | Low | 🟡 Important |
| 5 | We can detect a retention lift cleanly enough to know the feature caused it | We ship, the number wobbles, and we can't tell signal from seasonality | Med | 🟡 Important |
| 6 | One PM + ~1.5 eng can ship a trustworthy AI feature *and* a retention fix in one quarter | We ship a thin version of both and neither lands | Med | 🟡 Important |

## The kill shot
**Weakest link:** Assumption #1 + #2 together — we have *assumed* the cause of week-2 churn is low perceived value, and assumed the fix should target the manager. We have not proven either.
**Why it's fragile:** The most common async-standup drop-off pattern is contributor-side and habit-based: people stop because nothing forced the habit to stick past the novelty of week 1, or because the team lead quietly stopped caring. None of those are fixed by a smarter manager digest — and a manager-facing feature is invisible to the contributor who's the one going quiet. We're treating a habit-formation / activation problem as a value-perception problem because the value-perception story is the one that justifies the feature we already wanted to build.
**If it breaks:** We spend the quarter shipping the Blocker Detector, week-2 retention doesn't move, and we've burned the Q3 narrative *and* the renewal story on a misdiagnosis. The feature itself might be fine — but it was sold as a retention fix, so it reads as a failure.

## Pre-mortem
**It's Q4 and this failed. The headline:** "We shipped the Blocker Detector, managers liked it, and week-2 retention didn't budge."
**Most probable failure path:**
1. We don't instrument *why* teams drop in week 2; we assume "low value."
2. We build the Detector as the retention fix and the headline feature at once.
3. It lands as a manager-facing flag — accurate-ish, demo-friendly.
4. The teams that were going to churn had already stopped reading by day 8; they never see it.
5. Retention is flat. Seasonality + a concurrent pricing email make the number un-attributable anyway.
6. Leadership concludes "the AI bet didn't work," and the genuinely-useful feature inherits the stink of a retention miss it was never the right tool for.

## Metrics under pressure
- **Gameable / vanity metric:** "week-2 retention went up after launch." A before/after delta is confounded by seasonality, the concurrent onboarding-email change, and audience mix (who signed up that month). We could hit it and still have a feature that did nothing — or miss it and have a feature that worked.
- **The metric that would actually prove this worked:** a **holdout test** — randomly withhold the Detector from a matched cohort and compare week-2 check-in retention between exposed and control teams. For the *feature's own* value, track manager digest-open rate and blocker-flag accept/dismiss rate (do managers act on the flags, or dismiss them?).

## Holes, sorted
**🔴 Fatal (rethink):**
- **Root cause is assumed, not proven.** → **Fix / test:** Before building anything, pull the actual week-2 drop-off curve from the analyst — segment by *who* goes silent (whole team vs. trailing individuals) and *when*. One week of analysis. If it's a habit/activation problem, the fix is a week-1 nudge cadence, not an AI feature.
- **Fix targets the wrong user.** → **Fix / test:** Check whether churning teams ever opened a digest. If they didn't, a manager-facing feature cannot be the retention lever — decouple the two bets immediately.

**🟡 Serious (fix before shipping):**
- **No clean proof of causation.** → **Fix / test:** Gate the launch on a holdout/A-B test, not a before/after number. Define the exposed-vs-control comparison before writing code.
- **Detector accuracy is unmeasured.** → **Fix / test:** Hand-label ~150 recent check-ins for "real blocker / not" and measure precision before exposing flags to managers. Set a precision floor (e.g. ≥80%) below which we don't ship the flag UI.
- **Capacity is overcommitted.** → **Fix / test:** With ~1.5 eng for the quarter, pick *one* bet. Recommend: ship the cheap retention experiment first (week-1 nudge), build the Detector as a manager-value feature on its own track and timeline.

**⚪ Minor (note and move on):**
- The plan doesn't say what a "blocker" is in product terms — define the surface and the manager action before design starts.
- "AI Blocker Detector" is an internal framing; keep it out of customer-facing copy until accuracy is proven.

## If I had to save this plan
Split the two bets. Run a one-week analysis to find the *real* week-2 drop-off mechanism, and fix that with the cheapest matching intervention (most likely a week-1 nudge cadence, not AI). Build the Blocker Detector on its own track as a manager-value feature — measured by flag-accept rate and digest opens — and gate its launch on a holdout test plus a measured precision floor, not a before/after retention number. That moves the strategy from RETHINK to SHIP and keeps the feature honest.
