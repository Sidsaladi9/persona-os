# Week-2 retention opportunity solution tree

**TL;DR:** We mapped one outcome — lift week-2 team retention from 41% to 55% — to the customer problems behind why teams stop checking in after week 1. The riskiest problem isn't a missing feature; it's that the first week feels like a one-way chore with no payoff. 🟡 We're targeting "I'm checking in but nothing happens with it" and testing the cheapest fix (a personalized week-1 digest recap) before building anything.

## Inputs we used

**Target outcome:** Week-2 retention (% of teams still posting check-ins in days 8–14 after kickoff) currently 41%, target 55% by end of Q3.

**Research drawn on (real notes, harvested — not invented):**
- 11 onboarding interviews (Apr–May 2026) with EMs/team leads whose teams churned in week 2.
- 38 Intercom tickets tagged `setup` / `engagement` since March.
- Mixpanel funnel: 41% of teams that complete week 1 still post in week 2; member-level posting drops from 78% (day 2) to 34% (day 9).
- 3 win-back call notes from Sales (teams that downgraded to "just Slack").

**Verbatims that became opportunities:**
- *"My team filled it out for a week and then asked me what the point was. I didn't have a great answer."* — Priya R., EM, 14-person team
- *"I never actually read the digests. They pile up in a channel nobody opens."* — Marcus T., Eng lead
- *"Half my devs forgot. There's no nudge if you don't open Slack that morning."* — Dana K., team lead
- *"Setup took me 25 minutes and I was the only one who configured anything. The team just got dropped into it."* — Jon J., EM
- *"It felt like a status report for my benefit, not theirs. They got nothing back."* — Priya R.

## The tree

```
OUTCOME: Lift week-2 team retention from 41% to 55% by end of Q3

├─ OPPORTUNITY (theme): "Setup is on one person, the team is just dropped in"
│   ├─ "I configured everything; my team never understood why we adopted this"
│   └─ "There's no shared kickoff moment — week 1 has no team buy-in"
│
├─ OPPORTUNITY (theme): "I forget to check in and nothing reminds me in time"
│   ├─ "If I don't open Slack that morning, I miss the window entirely"
│   └─ "Reminders are the same for everyone; they don't adapt to when I work"
│
├─ OPPORTUNITY (theme): "The digest piles up unread; nobody acts on it"
│   ├─ "Digests land in a channel no one opens"
│   └─ "I can't tell at a glance what actually needs my attention as a manager"
│
└─ ★ TARGET OPPORTUNITY: "I'm checking in but nothing comes back — week 1 feels like a one-way chore with no payoff"
     ├─ SOLUTION 1: Personalized "your week in review" recap sent to each member at end of week 1 (what you shipped, what you unblocked, team momentum)
     │     └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: Concierge — hand-craft week-1 recaps for 12 new teams, measure week-2 posting vs. a matched 12-team control
     ├─ SOLUTION 2: Surface a per-member value moment inside the check-in flow ("you've logged 4 days — here's what your manager saw")
     │     └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: Fake-door — add the value-moment CTA to the check-in confirmation screen, measure click/expand rate
     ├─ SOLUTION 3: Reframe the digest as a two-way loop — managers react/acknowledge, members see their input was read
     │     └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: Prototype the "manager acknowledged your check-in" reply, usability-test with 5 EMs + 8 members
     └─ SOLUTION 4: Day-3 in-product "what teams get from check-ins" onboarding card with one peer benchmark stat
           └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: A/B the onboarding card on 50% of new teams, measure day-4 to day-9 retention delta
```

*The tree shows one LEAD experiment per solution. The full set of assumptions the leading solution rests on lives in the block below.*

## Target opportunity + why

We scored the four candidate themes on reach × impact × strategic fit (1–5 each):

| Opportunity | Reach (1–5) | Impact (1–5) | Strategic fit (1–5) | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ★ "Nothing comes back — week 1 is a one-way chore" | 5 | 5 | 5 | **15** |
| "Digest piles up unread; nobody acts on it" | 4 | 4 | 4 | 12 |
| "I forget to check in, no timely reminder" | 4 | 3 | 3 | 10 |
| "Setup is on one person, team just dropped in" | 3 | 3 | 3 | 9 |

**Choice:** "Nothing comes back — week 1 is a one-way chore." It scored highest on all three. **Reach (5):** the no-payoff feeling showed up in 8 of 11 interviews and is structural — every member experiences week 1, not a sub-segment. **Impact (5):** it's the root reason teams quit (the "what's the point?" conversation Priya described blocks the job entirely — the team stops valuing the ritual). **Strategic fit (5):** it ladders directly to week-2 retention — fixing the payoff is the most direct lever on whether members keep posting in days 8–14. The reminder and setup opportunities matter but are second-order: a better nudge to do a chore nobody values won't hold retention. They stay on the tree as next targets.

## Riskiest assumptions (leading solution — Solution 1, personalized week-1 recap)

- **Desirability — *riskiest*:** Members actually want a personal recap, and receiving it makes them keep checking in. If they shrug at it, the whole branch is dead. This is what the LEAD experiment tests.
- **Viability:** A retention lift here moves the $4/user/mo expansion math enough to justify ongoing investment — i.e., the recap saves teams that would otherwise churn to "just Slack."
- **Feasibility:** We can generate a genuinely useful recap from check-in data alone (shipped/unblocked/momentum) without new instrumentation. Risk is moderate — overlaps with the planned Blocker Detector data layer, so we may reuse it. Note: 1 of 3 engineers is on PTO 3 days this sprint, so feasibility scope must stay small.
- **Usability:** Members can read and get value from the recap in under 30 seconds, in the surface they already live in (Slack), without a new place to check.

## Next experiment

Run the **concierge week-1 recap** first — it's the cheapest test of the riskiest (desirability) assumption and needs zero engineering. We hand-build end-of-week-1 recaps for 12 newly onboarded teams using existing check-in data, deliver them in-channel, and compare week-2 posting against a matched 12-team control over two onboarding cohorts (~2 weeks).

- **Ship it if:** the recap cohort's week-2 retention beats control by ≥10 points (e.g., ~41% → ~51%+). Then we build Solution 1 for real.
- **Change the plan if:** retention is flat or members ignore the recap. That falsifies the desirability assumption, and we pivot to the two-way digest loop (Solution 3) — the "their input was read" mechanic — as the next test.

**No-research caveat:** opportunities here are harvested from 11 interviews + tickets + funnel data, so they're evidence-backed, not assumed. The peer-benchmark stat in Solution 4 is the one unvalidated input; confirm we can source a defensible benchmark before A/B-ing that card.
