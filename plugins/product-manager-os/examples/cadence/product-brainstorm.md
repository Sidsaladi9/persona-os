# product-brainstorm — worked example (Cadence)

> Generated by the Product Manager OS `product-brainstorm` skill. Demo company: Cadence.

---

**TL;DR**
- Week-2 retention is our worst leak: of teams that finish week 1, 38% stop checking in by the end of week 2. The drop is concentrated in teams where the manager never opens a digest.
- We diverged across 12 angles, pressure-tested the survivors, and converged on one bet: the digest is the retention engine, not the check-in. If managers don't visibly consume check-ins, members stop writing them.
- Riskiest assumption is testable from data we already have this week — no build required. Experiment design below.

## The session

You came in wanting to "fix week-2 retention." Below is the sparring session, not a summary of it. I pushed where you sounded most certain.

### 1. Problem (the thing we actually argued about)

You opened with "teams churn in week 2." I made you get specific. Here's where we landed:

- **Not "teams" — a person in a moment.** The person who churns is **a senior engineer on a 6-person team who gets the check-in prompt at 9:12am Tuesday of week 2, has nothing different to say than last week, and closes the Slack reminder without writing.** That's the moment. Not "onboarding," not "activation" — a specific 9am decision to skip.
- **Jobs-to-be-Done lens.** What does that engineer hire the check-in to do? In week 1: signal to a new manager that they're engaged. By week 2 that job is *done* — the manager has the signal. The check-in now has no job, so they fire it. That reframes everything: retention isn't a reminder problem, it's a **"why am I still doing this" problem.**
- **The number I made you check.** You quoted "38% drop." I asked where it concentrates. You didn't have it segmented, so we treated it as the first thing to pull (see experiment). My hypothesis, stated out loud so we can be wrong: the drop is near-zero on teams where the manager opens digests daily, and brutal on teams where the manager never opens one.

> The sharpest thing said this session: *we've been trying to retain the writer, but the writer's behavior is downstream of whether the reader shows up.*

### 2. Diverge — angles (generated with the gate open, no judging)

I deliberately included bad ones. The tenth is where the good one hides.

| # | Angle | Frame used |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Smarter reminders / better nudge copy | (the obvious one — listed to get past it) |
| 2 | Streaks + gamification for the writer | — |
| 3 | Make the **digest** the product: manager reacts/replies, member sees it landed | Inversion |
| 4 | "Skip today" as a first-class, guilt-free action that still logs presence | Constraint flip |
| 5 | Blocker Detector surfaces *one* at-risk item per team in week 2 → proves value early | 10x |
| 6 | Auto-draft the check-in from yesterday's commits/tickets; member edits, doesn't author | 10x |
| 7 | Manager-side onboarding: the manager, not the team, is the user we onboard | Inversion |
| 8 | Async standup → async *retro* in week 2 (different job, same surface) | JTBD |
| 9 | Analogy: Strava — you keep logging runs because the feed reacts, not because of a streak | Analogy |
| 10 | Analogy: a paper standup board — people stop because no one reads it, not because writing is hard | Analogy |
| 11 | Weekly digest-of-digests to the manager's manager → social pressure flows down | Second-order |
| 12 | Kill the daily cadence; default new teams to 2x/week so week-2 fatigue never starts | Constraint flip |

**Where I stopped:** angles 3, 9, 10 all point at the same root — **consumption drives contribution**. That convergence is a signal, not a coincidence. More angles past here would be avoidance.

### 3. Pressure-test (turned cold on the survivors)

Survivors: **#3 (digest as the product)**, **#5 (Blocker Detector as week-2 value proof)**, **#12 (lower default cadence)**.

**What would have to be true:**
- For #3: members must *notice* whether the manager engaged, and that noticing must change their behavior. If members write into a void and don't know it's a void, the digest isn't the lever.
- For #5: there must be a real at-risk item to flag in week 2. On a calm 6-person team there may be nothing — a detector that flags nothing looks broken.
- For #12: fatigue must be driven by *frequency*, not by *pointlessness*. If the job is gone, 2x/week is just a slower death.

**Pre-mortem — it's June 2027, week-2 retention didn't move. Why?**
- We shipped better nudges (#1). Open rates rose, check-ins didn't. We optimized the writer and ignored the reader. *(Most likely failure.)*
- We made the digest reactive, but managers — our actual buyers — are the ones who go quiet, and we never onboarded *them*. The void stayed a void.
- Blocker Detector flagged noise in week 2, eroded trust, and got muted.

**Riskiest assumption (the one that sinks everything):**
> Member check-in retention in week 2 is *caused* by whether the manager visibly consumes the digest. If retention is actually flat regardless of manager engagement, then "make the digest the product" is the wrong bet and we should chase cadence (#12) or the vanished job (#8) instead.

This is the belief we love because it's elegant. That's exactly why it needs the hardest push — and it's testable without building anything.

### 4. Converge

We stop here. One bet, one test.

## Output

- **Problem (sharpened):** A senior engineer on a small team, prompted at 9am on Tuesday of week 2, has nothing new to say and no sign anyone read last week's check-ins — so they close the reminder and stop. The job the check-in did in week 1 (signal engagement to a new manager) is already done.

- **Best ideas (ranked by upside × confidence):**
  1. 🟢 **Make the digest visibly consumed** — manager reactions/replies surface back to each member ("Priya read your check-in, flagged your blocker"). Closes the void. Highest upside, and cheap if the assumption holds.
  2. 🟡 **Onboard the manager, not just the team** — week-1 manager activation play, since manager silence is the suspected root cause.
  3. 🟡 **Lower default cadence to 2x/week for new teams** — removes week-2 fatigue, but only helps if frequency (not pointlessness) is the driver.

- **Riskiest assumption:** Week-2 member retention is *caused* by visible manager consumption of the digest. If it's flat regardless of manager engagement, idea #1 is wrong.

- **Next experiment to run (cheapest informative move — a data pull, not a build):**
  - **Do this week:** Pull every team that completed week 1 in the last 90 days. Segment by manager digest-open behavior in week 2 — bucket into *daily opener / occasional / never opened*. Cross with each team's week-2 member check-in completion rate. Shared analyst can run this; it's two joins on data we already log (digest opens + check-in events). No engineering needed.
  - **What would change my mind:** If never-open-manager teams retain within ~5 pts of daily-opener teams, the digest-consumption theory is dead — we pivot to cadence (#12) or the vanished-job angle (#8). If the gap is large (hypothesis: 25+ pts), idea #1 is confirmed as the bet and we scope the smallest "make consumption visible" prototype next sprint.
  - **Watch for confound:** engaged managers may correlate with already-healthy teams. If the data pull is suggestive but confounded, the *next* step is a 2-week holdback test — turn on visible manager reactions for half of new teams, not a correlational read.

- **Why this is the move:** It attacks the one belief that makes everything else irrelevant, it costs an analyst an afternoon instead of an engineer a sprint, and one of our three engineers is on PTO this sprint anyway — so spending build capacity before we've read the data would be the expensive mistake.
