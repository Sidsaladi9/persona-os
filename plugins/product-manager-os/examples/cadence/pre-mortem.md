# Pre-Mortem: AI Blocker Detector launch

**TL;DR**
We are about to ship Blocker Detector — an LLM feature that reads daily check-ins and flags at-risk work in the manager digest. The most likely ways it dies: it cries wolf (low precision), managers ignore the flags, or it leaks sensitive check-in text into a model we don't control. We've reordered the launch to gate on precision, ship behind a 50/50 holdout, and run a privacy review before any team sees a flag.

**Date of exercise:** 2026-06-17
**Success defined as:** By 2026-09-30 (end of Q3), ≥ 35% of digest-receiving managers act on a Blocker Detector flag at least once a week, AND flag precision ≥ 0.80 on the labeled eval set, with no increase in digest unsubscribe rate.
**Failure frame:** It is 2026-10-07. The launch failed — we shipped 4 weeks late, precision sat around 0.55 so managers learned to scroll past the "At risk" section, weekly action rate never crossed 12%, two teams filed data-handling complaints, and Sales paused two enterprise deals pending a security answer we couldn't give.
**Measurement & launch bar:** Pre-registered primary metric: % of digest-receiving managers who act on a flag (click "review" or resolve) ≥ 1×/week. Control/holdout design: 50/50 holdout at the workspace level — half of opted-in workspaces get flags in the digest, half get the digest unchanged; compare week-2 retention and unsubscribe across arms. Quality gate: precision ≥ 0.80 and recall ≥ 0.50 on a 300-item human-labeled eval set before any flag is shown to a real team; p95 added digest-generation latency < 8s.

## Top risks — mitigation & early warning

### Risk 1: Low precision destroys trust in the flags — Score 20
- **Why it's plausible:** Check-ins are short, informal, and ambiguous; "still grinding on the migration" reads as either fine or blocked. A naive prompt over that data over-flags, and a manager only needs to be wrong-flagged 2–3 times to tune the section out for good.
- **Mitigation:** Hold the launch behind the quality gate — no flag ships to a real team until precision ≥ 0.80 on the 300-item human-labeled eval set. Tune the prompt + threshold against that set, and ship conservative (flag only high-confidence cases) rather than maximize recall.
- **Early-warning signal:** Eval-set precision < 0.75 at the 2026-08-22 model checkpoint (5+ weeks before deadline), OR in-product "was this flag useful?" thumbs-down rate > 30% in the first dogfood week.
- **Tripwire → response:** If precision is < 0.75 at the Aug 22 checkpoint, do NOT ship flags to teams — ship the digest section in "shadow mode" (logged, not shown) and slip the customer-facing launch rather than ship a noisy model.
- **Owner:** Priya (PM) + shared data analyst · **Check by:** 2026-08-22

### Risk 2: Check-in text leaks into a third-party LLM with no data-handling answer — Score 15
- **Why it's plausible:** Check-ins routinely contain customer names, incident detail, and occasionally comp talk. Our enterprise buyers (20–200 person tech cos) have security reviews, and "where does our text go" is the first question. We have no signed DPA with the model vendor yet.
- **Mitigation:** Run a privacy + security review before any flag is generated on real data: confirm a zero-retention / no-training inference path, sign the DPA, add a workspace-level opt-in toggle (off by default for enterprise), and write the one-page data-flow doc Sales can hand to security teams.
- **Early-warning signal:** DPA still unsigned or zero-retention path unconfirmed by 2026-08-08, OR any inbound security question from a team that we can't answer in writing.
- **Tripwire → response:** If the DPA/zero-retention path isn't confirmed by Aug 8, the feature ships opt-in and disabled for all enterprise workspaces until it is — no exceptions.
- **Owner:** Priya (PM) + eng lead (Marcus) · **Check by:** 2026-08-08

### Risk 3: Correct flags still drive no action (no clear next step) — Score 16
- **Why it's plausible:** A flag that says "this work looks at risk" with no affordance leaves the manager to figure out what to do. Managers are busy; an observation without a one-click action gets read and dropped, so even a precise model produces a flat action rate.
- **Mitigation:** Design each flag with a built-in next action — "Nudge [teammate]", "Add to 1:1", or "Mark resolved" — directly in the digest. Run 5 manager interviews before build to confirm the action set; instrument click-through on each action.
- **Early-warning signal:** In the dogfood/holdout, flag → action click-through < 20% in week 1, OR managers in interviews can't articulate what they'd do with a flag.
- **Tripwire → response:** If week-1 action click-through is < 20%, pause broader rollout and rework the action affordances before expanding past the holdout — do not scale a section nobody acts on.
- **Owner:** Jordan (designer) + Priya (PM) · **Check by:** 2026-09-05

### Risk 4: Precision gate isn't ready in time; we ship late or unvalidated — Score 12
- **Why it's plausible:** One of three engineers is on PTO for 3 days this sprint, and the eval-labeling effort (300 items, human-judged) is easy to underestimate. If labeling slips, the gate has no data and the team is tempted to ship on vibes against the Q3 deadline.
- **Mitigation:** Start eval-set labeling in week 1 (not after the model is built); split labeling across PM + analyst so it isn't on the critical eng path. Cut Q3 scope to a single flag type (stalled work) rather than the full taxonomy to protect the timeline.
- **Early-warning signal:** Fewer than 150 of 300 eval items labeled by 2026-08-01, OR the stalled-work model isn't feature-complete by 2026-08-15.
- **Tripwire → response:** If labeling is behind at Aug 1, the PM picks up labeling directly and we formally drop the second flag type from Q3 scope to hold the date.
- **Owner:** Priya (PM) · **Check by:** 2026-08-01

### Risk 5: Inference cost/latency breaks the digest SLA and the unit economics — Score 12
- **Why it's plausible:** A per-check-in LLM call across every team member, every day, adds real latency and cost. At $4/user/mo, an unbounded model call per digest can erase the margin and push p95 digest generation well past acceptable.
- **Mitigation:** Batch check-ins per digest into a single call, cache where text is unchanged, set a hard token budget, and measure cost-per-digest in staging before launch. Pre-register p95 added latency < 8s and a cost-per-active-user ceiling.
- **Early-warning signal:** Staging cost-per-digest above the modeled ceiling, OR p95 added latency > 8s in load testing.
- **Tripwire → response:** If either ceiling is breached, drop to once-daily batch scoring (not real-time) and/or a cheaper model tier before launch rather than absorbing the margin hit.
- **Owner:** Marcus (eng lead) · **Check by:** 2026-08-29

## Watch list (monitored, not actively managed)
- Sparse-check-in teams get no value — early-warning signal: < 10% of flags fire on teams below the median check-in volume.
- Bias toward terse writers creates IC friction — early-warning signal: thumbs-down flags cluster on a small set of quiet team members.
- Launching on top of unfixed week-2 retention — early-warning signal: holdout teams churn before digest #4 in both arms.
- Geekbot/Standuply ship a comparable flag first — early-warning signal: competitor changelog or sales-call mention of a "risk" feature.
- Holdout contaminated by user-level split — early-warning signal: control-arm managers report seeing flags.

## Plan changes triggered by this pre-mortem
- Added a hard precision gate (≥ 0.80 on a 300-item labeled eval set) as a launch blocker; flags ship in shadow mode if the gate isn't met by Aug 22.
- Moved the privacy/security review and DPA sign-off **before** any real-data inference; feature ships opt-in and off by default for enterprise until confirmed.
- Cut Q3 scope from the full flag taxonomy to a single flag type (stalled work) to protect the timeline given the PTO gap.
- Each flag must carry a built-in next action (nudge / add to 1:1 / resolve) — no observation-only flags.
- Set the holdout at the **workspace** level, not user level, to keep the control arm clean.
