# Product Strategy — Cadence (Product Team) — 2026-06-17

**TL;DR:** We win async standups not by being a better Geekbot, but by making the *digest* the product managers actually rely on — and the wedge is the Blocker Detector. Next year we go all-in on engineering managers at 20-200 person tech companies, fix the week-2 habit gap that's quietly capping our growth, and deliberately walk away from non-eng teams and the "config-it-yourself bot" game. Status: 🟡 at risk until week-2 retention moves.

## Vision

A year from now, an engineering manager opens Cadence's morning digest and trusts it more than a live standup — because it surfaces the at-risk work and stalled threads a meeting would have buried. The async check-in becomes the team's default operating rhythm, not a tool they forget by week two.

## Success looks like

The two outcomes the bets below are accountable for:

- **Week-2 team retention: 41% → 60% within 2 quarters** (share of teams still checking in 14 days after activation). This is the single metric that gates everything else.
- **Net revenue retention: 102% → 115% within 4 quarters**, driven by Blocker Detector attach and per-seat expansion inside retained teams.

## Diagnosis (the challenge)

The hard truth: **Cadence is easy to start and easy to abandon.** We've added ~4,000 paying teams and ~$3M ARR, but the funnel leaks at the habit layer, not the signup layer.

- **The crux — the week-2 cliff.** ~38% of teams that activate in week 1 stop checking in by day 14. The check-in is a chore with no payoff for the *individual* answering it; the value (the digest) accrues to the manager, who isn't the one filling it out. When 3 of 5 engineers skip a day, the manager sees a thin digest, decides it's not worth chasing, and the team quietly churns. This is the bottleneck — most of our lost ARR dies here, before any pricing or feature debate matters.
- **What changed.** "Just use Slack" got better (Slack now ships native workflow reminders), and Geekbot is competing hard on price. Parity on the check-in mechanic is now table stakes — being a slightly nicer form-filler is not a position worth defending.
- **What most people are avoiding saying:** our growth has been masking the retention problem. New-team volume covers for the leak. The moment acquisition slows, the cliff shows up in NRR. We are one funnel quarter away from this being visible to the board.

## Where we play

- **Focus: engineering managers and team leads at 20-200 person tech companies.** This is where the digest's value is highest (enough people to make async worth it, few enough that the manager personally feels a thin digest) and where "Slack alone" breaks down first.
- **Focus: the daily/standup-replacement use case**, specifically teams that have *already given up* on synchronous standups or want to — our strongest converting wedge.
- **Not playing (this year):** non-engineering teams (sales, marketing, HR async check-ins), sub-10-person teams (digest has too little signal), and 500+ person enterprises requiring heavy SSO/procurement cycles. We'll serve the upper edge of our range opportunistically but won't build *for* it.

## How we win

**We win on the digest, not the check-in — and the Blocker Detector is the wedge that makes the digest indispensable.** Anyone can collect async answers (Geekbot, Slack, Standuply do). What rivals can't easily copy is a digest that turns a pile of check-ins into a ranked list of *at-risk work* a manager would otherwise miss.

Our defensible advantage: **we sit on the longitudinal check-in corpus** — months of per-person, per-team check-in history that competitors positioned as lightweight bots don't retain or model. Blocker Detector is trained on that history (what a stall looks like *for this team*), so its at-risk flags get sharper the longer a team stays. That's a compounding moat: switching to Geekbot means starting the detector cold. "Better UX" is not our how-to-win; "a digest that gets smarter the longer you stay" is.

## Strategic pillars (3–4)

1. **Close the week-2 cliff — make the check-in pay off for the person answering it.** Reduce check-in friction (sub-30-second mobile flow, smart pre-fill from linked work) and give the *individual* a reason to show up (personalized "your blockers / your wins" recap, not just a manager digest). **Decision gate:** if week-2 retention isn't ≥ 52% within 1 quarter, we stop adding surface area and run a dedicated habit-loop redesign before anything else ships.

2. **Ship Blocker Detector as the digest's headline, not a side feature.** Make at-risk-work flagging the first thing a manager sees in every digest, with one-tap follow-up. This is both the how-to-win mechanism and the expansion lever. **Decision gate:** if manager weekly-active on the digest doesn't rise and Blocker Detector flags aren't acted on ≥ 25% of the time by end of Q2, we revisit the model before scaling it to all teams.

3. **Earn the moat — invest in the check-in data layer.** Treat the longitudinal corpus as the core asset: per-team baselines, stall detection that improves with tenure, and a "Cadence gets smarter the longer you stay" narrative across product and onboarding. This is what makes pillars 1 and 2 defensible rather than copyable.

4. **Re-anchor pricing on the digest's value, not seat count (pilot, not commitment).** Test a value tier where Blocker Detector + manager analytics drive expansion, so NRR isn't capped by team headcount. **Decision gate:** pilot with ≤ 50 teams; only roll out if it lifts NRR without depressing activation.

## What we're NOT doing

- **Not expanding to non-engineering teams this year.** Trade-off: we leave sales/marketing/HR async-check-in TAM on the table. Why: our digest value and Blocker Detector moat are built on engineering work signals; chasing horizontal breadth now would dilute the one place we can be undeniably better and starve the retention fix.
- **Not competing on price against Geekbot.** Trade-off: we'll lose some price-sensitive small teams. Why: a price war erases the margin we need to fund the data/ML investment that *is* our moat. We compete on the digest being worth more, not costing less.
- **Not building the deep enterprise feature set (advanced SSO, audit, custom roles) this year.** Trade-off: we defer 500+ seat deals and the ARR they'd bring. Why: those deals lengthen the cycle and pull a 5-person team toward procurement work instead of the week-2 fix that unlocks the whole funnel.
- **Not adding new check-in *formats* or integrations as a growth lever.** Trade-off: feels like we're standing still on surface area. Why: more formats deepen the "easy to start, easy to abandon" trap. Depth on retention beats breadth on collection.

## Why this is coherent (the logic)

The crux is the week-2 cliff, and every piece points at it. **Pillar 1** attacks the cliff directly by making the check-in worth the answerer's time. But fixing friction alone won't hold teams if the digest still isn't worth the manager's attention — so **Pillar 2** makes the digest indispensable via Blocker Detector, which is also our how-to-win. Both only stay defensible because **Pillar 3** turns our one rivals-can't-copy asset (the longitudinal corpus) into a detector that compounds with tenure — directly rewarding the retention Pillar 1 buys. **Pillar 4** then converts that retained, higher-value usage into NRR that headcount-based pricing was capping. Where-we-play (eng managers, standup-replacement) is exactly where the digest's signal is richest and where the moat is strongest; the non-goals all protect the focus and the margin that fund the moat. Diagnosis → where/how → pillars read as one chain: *retained teams feed the corpus → the corpus sharpens the detector → the detector makes the digest indispensable → indispensability drives retention and expansion.*

## Key risks

- **Must be true: the digest, not the check-in, is what teams will pay more for.** If managers don't value Blocker Detector enough to anchor pricing on it, Pillar 4 collapses. Early check: instrument digest weekly-active and Blocker Detector flag-action rate within the first 6 weeks; treat low action rate as an early kill signal.
- **Must be true: friction reduction actually moves week-2 retention.** If the cliff is motivational (managers not chasing) rather than effort-based (forms too long), Pillar 1's friction work won't fix it. Check: run the habit-loop redesign decision gate at 1 quarter and don't assume the cause.
- **Capacity risk: a 5-person product team (1 engineer on PTO 3 days this sprint) cannot run four pillars in parallel.** If we spread thin, we recreate the exact "win everywhere" failure this strategy rejects. Mitigation: Pillar 1 is the non-negotiable Now; Pillars 3–4 are sequenced behind it.
- **Competitive risk: Slack or Geekbot ships a credible "at-risk work" digest before our corpus advantage compounds.** Our defense is tenure-dependent, so the moat is weak early. Check: track Blocker Detector accuracy lift by team tenure quarterly; if it's not improving with age, the moat thesis is wrong and we rethink how-to-win.
