**TL;DR**
- We're losing teams in week 2: of 312 teams that activated in Apr–May, 41% stopped checking in after day 7, and only 34% were still checking in at day 21. Surveys tell us "got busy" — that's a polite lie, not a cause.
- This is a churn/retention interview, so we use the inverted Mom Test frame: anchor on the last real check-in and the exact week the rhythm broke, not on a generic problem.
- Below: the interview plan, a non-leading script, and a note template. Run 6 interviews, then hand notes to `synthesize-research`.

This is problem discovery, not a save attempt. We do not pitch the Blocker Detector or defend the product when a team describes drifting away. We want the honest story of why check-ins stopped earning their time.

## Interview plan

```
LEARNING GOAL: Find the specific moment and trigger in week 2 when a team's
  daily check-in rhythm breaks — who drifts first, what changed that week, and
  what (if anything) filled the gap — so we know whether week-2 churn is a
  habit-formation problem, a manager-value problem, or a notification/timing problem.

RISKIEST ASSUMPTION: I believe teams stop checking in because the daily check-in
  feels like low-value busywork once the novelty wears off (i.e. the digest isn't
  visibly changing how the manager runs the team); if false — if the real driver
  is mechanical (bad reminder timing, Slack notifications muted, no manager
  follow-up) — then our roadmap bet on richer digest value (Blocker Detector) is
  aimed at the wrong cause and week-2 retention won't move.

SEGMENT: Engineering teams of 5–12 people at 20–200-person tech companies that
  activated in the last 60 days and dropped from daily check-ins between day 7 and
  day 21 — chosen because this is exactly the week-2 cliff we're trying to fix, and
  recently-churned teams still remember the specifics (older churn = fuzzy memory).

WHO (same plan reused across the segment; 6 interviews):
  - Maya Chen — EM, 8-person platform team @ Brightloop (Series B, ~90 ppl).
    Activated Apr 14, strong week 1 (7/8 checking in daily), collapsed to 2/8 by Apr 24.
  - Diego Ramos — Team lead, 6-person team @ Nimbus Freight (~140 ppl). Activated
    May 2; he personally stopped writing check-ins around May 12 while team continued ~1 wk longer.
  - Priya Nair — EM, 11-person team @ Cartwheel (~60 ppl). Migrated off Geekbot to
    Cadence Apr 28; reverted to "just Slack" by mid-May.
  - Tom Bauer — Founding eng / acting lead, 5-person team @ Joro Labs (~25 ppl, seed→A).
    Activated May 5, never got past 3 consecutive days in week 2.
  - Aisha Okoye — EM, 9-person team @ Sundeck (~110 ppl). Two ICs went on PTO in week 2;
    rhythm never recovered after they returned.
  - Wes Patterson — Director of Eng overseeing 3 teams @ Halberd Systems (~180 ppl).
    Rolled out top-down to 22 people; only 1 of 3 teams stuck past week 2.

LOGISTICS: 30 min each · recording consent? y (ask at top, note in template) ·
  notetaker: PM runs + takes notes; analyst (shared) reviews recordings async for the 2 richest.

SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE: For each team, a dated week-2 moment ("Tuesday the 22nd, after
  standup came back") tied to a concrete trigger, the name/role of whoever drifted
  first, and what they do now instead of the check-in. If 4+ of 6 point to the same
  trigger class, we have our root cause and can stop guessing.
```

## Question script

Non-leading, grounded in past behavior, with optional probe follow-ups. We use the retention / churn variant of the problem block — anchored on the last real check-in, not a hypothetical. Probes are depth to reach for when an answer is rich, not a checklist to grind through.

```
WARM-UP
- Quick consent check: okay if I record so I'm not heads-down typing? I'll stop anytime.
- Tell me a bit about your team — size, what you're shipping right now, how a typical week runs.

CONTEXT
- Before Cadence, how did your team stay in sync day to day — standups, Slack, something else?
  ↳ probe: Who ran that, and what was it actually for — status, unblocking, visibility for you?
  ↳ probe: What made you try moving to async written check-ins in the first place?

PROBLEM STORY  (retention / churn variant — inverted frame)
- Walk me through the last time someone on your team actually wrote a check-in.
  ↳ probe: When was the time before that? (find where the cadence thinned out)
  ↳ probe: Which week did it stop being part of the routine — and what changed that week
    specifically? (push for a date/event, not "we got busy")
  ↳ probe: Who stopped first — you or someone on the team — and what did the rest do after that?
  ↳ probe: What did you do with the digests while people were still checking in — open them,
    skim, act on something? Tell me about the last digest you remember reading.
  ↳ probe (watch for emotion): Was there a moment it started to feel like a chore? When?

CURRENT WORKAROUNDS
- Now that the check-ins have tailed off, how does your team stay in sync instead?
  ↳ probe: Walk me through how you found out about a blocker or slipping task this past week.
  ↳ probe: Did you go back to standup, lean on Slack, 1:1s, a board — what filled the gap?
  ↳ probe: Did you ever pay for or set up anything to fix the sync problem before Cadence?

WRAP
- What's the hardest part of keeping a team in sync, for you specifically, and why?
- Who else dealt with this drop-off that I should talk to — an IC who stopped early, another lead?
- Anything I should have asked about why the check-ins stopped, but didn't?
```

## Notes for the interviewer

- 🟡 We're at risk of hearing "we just got busy" six times. That's not an answer — push for the dated week-2 moment and the specific trigger every time.
- Find who drifted first. In a team, one person usually goes quiet before the rhythm collapses; "who stopped first?" surfaces the real failure mode (manager vs. IC).
- Stay out of pitch mode. When a team describes leaving, do not defend the digest or tease the Blocker Detector — that turns honesty into politeness.
- Commitment over compliments: if a team went back to paying for or rebuilding standup, that tells us what job we stopped doing well.
- Take notes in their words. Verbatim quotes are what `synthesize-research` clusters on.

## Research Synthesis: Why teams stop checking in during week 2

**Research question:** Why do many teams that adopt Cadence in week 1 stop checking in during week 2 — and what should we build/change to fix week-2 retention?

**Method & sample:** User interviews · n=5 · recruited from the Amplitude "no check-in 5+ days, days 8-14" cohort · Segments: 5 churned-week-2 admins/leads; team sizes 6-22; 1 ex-Geekbot evaluator, 1 partially co-located team. **Caveat: n=5, all admin-side, no individual-member interviews — directional, not conclusive.**

## Top themes

| Theme | Frequency | Impact | Representative quote |
|-------|-----------|--------|----------------------|
| "Nothing keeps the habit going once I stop reminding people" | 5/5 | 🔴 Blocker — churned segment | "No nudge from the product, no nudge from me, so it stopped." — co-located lead |
| "A half-empty digest looks broken, so I stop opening it" | 4/5 | 🔴 Blocker — churned segment | "Day 8 it was 4/15 and a half-empty digest is depressing." — 15-person EM |
| "One global 9am prompt doesn't fit my team's hours/timezones" | 3/5 | 🟡 High — split-team + ex-Geekbot | "Half the company gets pinged at 1am." — ex-Geekbot evaluator |
| "I can't tell what Cadence gives me over our Slack channel" | 2/5 | 🟡 High — value-perception | "Couldn't tell what Cadence was giving me that the standup channel wasn't." — 8-person lead |
| "Adding the team is too manual" | 2/5 | 🟡 Med — onboarding friction | "15 people one at a time… took me half an hour." — 15-person EM |
| "Async feels redundant when we're co-located" | 1/5 | 🟢 Low — niche segment | "When we're co-located the async check-in feels redundant." — 6-person lead |

**Also raised (residual):** prompt-time setting hard to find (P1); members who never connected still get pinged (P2); desire for blocker flagging "before Sam says anything out loud" (P2, P4 — maps to Blocker Detector).

## Key insights

1. **Week-2 churn is a habit-decay problem, not a feature-gap problem — the product never re-engages, so the habit dies when the human reminder does.**
   - Observation: 5/5 admins said participation held in week 1 *because they manually reminded the team*, and decayed in week 2 *once they stopped*. None reported the product nudging anyone.
   - Interpretation (hypothesis): Cadence currently outsources the retention loop to a human admin; when that admin's attention lapses (which it does by week 2), there is no system-side fallback, so the team drifts.
   - Evidence: theme 1 (5/5); "no nudge from the product, no nudge from me, so it stopped" (P4); "I forgot to remind them and it just died" (P1).
   - So what: This is the single highest-leverage fix for week-2 retention — it's the only theme that is unanimous AND a blocker. Auto-reminders / non-responder nudges likely move the metric more than any net-new feature.
   - Confidence: High for the pattern (5/5), Directional on the fix (no experiment yet).

2. **The digest's value collapses below a participation threshold — low fill rate and a thin digest reinforce each other into a death spiral.**
   - Observation: 4/5 said a half-/mostly-empty digest looked "broken," "depressing," or "thin," and that this was the moment they stopped opening it.
   - Interpretation (hypothesis): The digest has an implicit participation floor; below it, the core artifact degrades faster than linearly and becomes a *negative* signal ("the tool is failing") rather than a neutral one.
   - Evidence: theme 2 (4/5); "a half-empty digest is depressing… so I stopped opening it" (P5); "it just restated what people typed" (P2).
   - So what: Reinforces insight 1 (fill rate is the lever) and suggests the digest should degrade gracefully — summarize what *is* there as useful rather than displaying gaps as failure.
   - Confidence: High (4/5).

3. **Scheduling rigidity is a real but secondary driver, concentrated in split/distributed teams.**
   - Observation: 3/5 hit friction with the single global 9am prompt; for the ex-Geekbot evaluator (P3) it was the explicit reason Cadence couldn't replace her current tool.
   - Interpretation (hypothesis): A global prompt time is fine for co-located teams but a hard blocker for timezone-split teams, and it's also a competitive parity gap vs Geekbot (per-person schedules).
   - Evidence: theme 3 (3/5); "half the company gets pinged at 1am" (P3); P1's 9am-fires-before-team-is-online complaint.
   - So what: Won't fix week-2 churn for the majority, but it's table-stakes for distributed teams and a competitive loss point — fix after the retention loop.
   - Confidence: Directional, n=3.

## Surprises & contradictions

- **The digest is loved and blamed in the same breath.** P1 called it "the thing I'd have written myself"; P5 stopped opening it. These don't contradict — they confirm insight 2: the *same* digest is great at high fill rate and worthless at low fill rate. The variable is participation, not the digest itself.
- **Disconfirming the "they didn't get value" hypothesis:** none of the 5 churned because the product was bad. P4 said it plainly: "I didn't churn because it's bad… we drifted." This pushes against a feature-quality read of week-2 churn and toward the habit-loop read (insight 1).
- **Blocker Detector demand showed up unprompted** (P2, P4) as the thing that would have made them *stay/pay* — useful tailwind for priority (2), though n=2.

## Recommendations (ranked)

1. **Ship product-driven re-engagement: auto-remind the team and DM non-responders** — addresses theme 1 (habit decay). **Ship now.** Unanimous blocker, no human-replaceable workaround, and it directly attacks the week-2 metric. Pair with an admin "participation dropping" alert. (Watch the 3-day PTO this sprint — scope to reminders + non-responder nudge, defer the admin alert if needed.)
2. **Make the digest degrade gracefully below a participation floor** — addresses theme 2. **Ship now (small).** Summarize present check-ins as useful; replace empty-state "gaps" with a non-punitive "3 of 15 checked in" framing rather than a broken-looking digest.
3. **Add per-person / per-timezone prompt scheduling** — addresses theme 3. **Investigate then ship.** Table-stakes vs Geekbot for distributed teams; size it after (1)–(2) land. Quick adjacent win: surface the prompt-time setting (P1 couldn't find it).
4. **Sharpen the "vs your Slack channel" value story via Blocker Detector** — addresses theme 4 + residual blocker-flag demand. **Investigate.** Two admins named "flag who's stuck before they say it" as the stay-and-pay feature; aligns with the planned Blocker Detector launch.
5. **Streamline bulk team onboarding** — addresses theme 5. **Investigate.** Lower-frequency (2/5) but cheap; bulk Slack import removes a half-hour admin tax.

## Open questions / research next

- **We only heard from admins.** Why do *individual members* stop replying in week 2 — fatigue, prompt timing, or "no one reads it anyway"? Need 5-6 member-side interviews.
- **Is there a participation floor where the digest dies?** Pull Amplitude: digest-open rate vs % of team checked in — find the threshold to inform recommendation 2.
- **Would auto-reminders have saved these teams, or just annoyed them?** Run a reminder/non-responder-nudge experiment on the next trial cohort and measure week-2 check-in rate vs control.
- **How big is the co-located-redundancy segment?** Only 1/5 here; quantify before treating it as a real churn driver.

# Week-2 retention opportunity solution tree

**TL;DR:** We mapped one outcome — lift week-2 team retention from 41% to 55% — to the customer problems behind why teams stop checking in after week 1. The riskiest problem isn't a missing feature; it's that the first week feels like a one-way chore with no payoff. 🟡 We're targeting "I'm checking in but nothing happens with it" and testing the cheapest fix (a personalized week-1 digest recap) before building anything.

## Inputs we used

**Target outcome:** Week-2 retention (% of teams still posting check-ins in days 8–14 after kickoff) currently 41%, target 55% by end of Q3.

**Research drawn on (real notes, harvested — not invented):**
- 11 onboarding interviews (Apr–May 2026) with EMs/team leads whose teams churned in week 2.
- 38 Intercom tickets tagged `setup` / `engagement` since March.
- Mixpanel funnel: 41% of teams that complete week 1 still post in week 2; member-level posting drops from 78% (day 2) to 34% (day 9).
- 3 win-back call notes from Sales (teams that downgraded to "just Slack").

**Verbatims that became opportunities:**
- *"My team filled it out for a week and then asked me what the point was. I didn't have a great answer."* — Priya R., EM, 14-person team
- *"I never actually read the digests. They pile up in a channel nobody opens."* — Marcus T., Eng lead
- *"Half my devs forgot. There's no nudge if you don't open Slack that morning."* — Dana K., team lead
- *"Setup took me 25 minutes and I was the only one who configured anything. The team just got dropped into it."* — Jon J., EM
- *"It felt like a status report for my benefit, not theirs. They got nothing back."* — Priya R.

## The tree

```
OUTCOME: Lift week-2 team retention from 41% to 55% by end of Q3

├─ OPPORTUNITY (theme): "Setup is on one person, the team is just dropped in"
│   ├─ "I configured everything; my team never understood why we adopted this"
│   └─ "There's no shared kickoff moment — week 1 has no team buy-in"
│
├─ OPPORTUNITY (theme): "I forget to check in and nothing reminds me in time"
│   ├─ "If I don't open Slack that morning, I miss the window entirely"
│   └─ "Reminders are the same for everyone; they don't adapt to when I work"
│
├─ OPPORTUNITY (theme): "The digest piles up unread; nobody acts on it"
│   ├─ "Digests land in a channel no one opens"
│   └─ "I can't tell at a glance what actually needs my attention as a manager"
│
└─ ★ TARGET OPPORTUNITY: "I'm checking in but nothing comes back — week 1 feels like a one-way chore with no payoff"
     ├─ SOLUTION 1: Personalized "your week in review" recap sent to each member at end of week 1 (what you shipped, what you unblocked, team momentum)
     │     └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: Concierge — hand-craft week-1 recaps for 12 new teams, measure week-2 posting vs. a matched 12-team control
     ├─ SOLUTION 2: Surface a per-member value moment inside the check-in flow ("you've logged 4 days — here's what your manager saw")
     │     └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: Fake-door — add the value-moment CTA to the check-in confirmation screen, measure click/expand rate
     ├─ SOLUTION 3: Reframe the digest as a two-way loop — managers react/acknowledge, members see their input was read
     │     └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: Prototype the "manager acknowledged your check-in" reply, usability-test with 5 EMs + 8 members
     └─ SOLUTION 4: Day-3 in-product "what teams get from check-ins" onboarding card with one peer benchmark stat
           └─ LEAD EXPERIMENT: A/B the onboarding card on 50% of new teams, measure day-4 to day-9 retention delta
```

## Target opportunity + why

We scored the four candidate themes on reach × impact × strategic fit (1–5 each):

| Opportunity | Reach (1–5) | Impact (1–5) | Strategic fit (1–5) | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ★ "Nothing comes back — week 1 is a one-way chore" | 5 | 5 | 5 | **15** |
| "Digest piles up unread; nobody acts on it" | 4 | 4 | 4 | 12 |
| "I forget to check in, no timely reminder" | 4 | 3 | 3 | 10 |
| "Setup is on one person, team just dropped in" | 3 | 3 | 3 | 9 |

**Choice:** "Nothing comes back — week 1 is a one-way chore." It scored highest on all three. **Reach (5):** the no-payoff feeling showed up in 8 of 11 interviews and is structural — every member experiences week 1, not a sub-segment. **Impact (5):** it's the root reason teams quit (the "what's the point?" conversation Priya described blocks the job entirely — the team stops valuing the ritual). **Strategic fit (5):** it ladders directly to week-2 retention — fixing the payoff is the most direct lever on whether members keep posting in days 8–14. The reminder and setup opportunities matter but are second-order: a better nudge to do a chore nobody values won't hold retention. They stay on the tree as next targets.

## Riskiest assumptions (leading solution — Solution 1, personalized week-1 recap)

- **Desirability — *riskiest*:** Members actually want a personal recap, and receiving it makes them keep checking in. If they shrug at it, the whole branch is dead. This is what the LEAD experiment tests.
- **Viability:** A retention lift here moves the $4/user/mo expansion math enough to justify ongoing investment — i.e., the recap saves teams that would otherwise churn to "just Slack."
- **Feasibility:** We can generate a genuinely useful recap from check-in data alone (shipped/unblocked/momentum) without new instrumentation. Risk is moderate — overlaps with the planned Blocker Detector data layer, so we may reuse it. Note: 1 of 3 engineers is on PTO 3 days this sprint, so feasibility scope must stay small.
- **Usability:** Members can read and get value from the recap in under 30 seconds, in the surface they already live in (Slack), without a new place to check.

## Next experiment

Run the **concierge week-1 recap** first — it's the cheapest test of the riskiest (desirability) assumption and needs zero engineering. We hand-build end-of-week-1 recaps for 12 newly onboarded teams using existing check-in data, deliver them in-channel, and compare week-2 posting against a matched 12-team control over two onboarding cohorts (~2 weeks).

- **Ship it if:** the recap cohort's week-2 retention beats control by ≥10 points (e.g., ~41% → ~51%+). Then we build Solution 1 for real.

# Segmentation: async standup market (teams running written check-ins)

**TL;DR**
- We segmented our 4,000 paying teams by the *job they hire async check-ins to do*, not by company size — because week-2 retention varies wildly by job, and company size barely predicts it.
- Beachhead: **Distributed-by-default teams** — they have the most acute pain (no hallway substitute), highest reachability, and our clearest right-to-win vs. Geekbot/Slack.

**Population segmented:** current 4,000 paying teams (~28,000 seats).
**Primary lens:** jobs-to-be-done — chosen because week-2 retention (our target metric) differs most by *why a team adopted async check-ins*, not by headcount or industry.
**Strategic constraint this cycle:** learning + retention — find the segment where we can most durably lift week-2 retention and use that as the launchpad for Blocker Detector.
**Data basis:** 18 win/loss interviews (Q1–Q2), Mixpanel cohort retention by self-reported "why you signed up" onboarding field (n≈3,100 teams answered), 600+ support tickets tagged by theme. Sizing leans on internal counts (`[sourced]`) plus one market analog (`[estimate]`).

## Decision and metric this must move

Decision: which segment to focus the week-2 retention fix *and* the Blocker Detector launch on first.
Metric: **week-2 retention** (teams with ≥3 check-ins in days 8–14 ÷ teams that finished week 1). Today blended ~52%. We chose the JTBD lens because the retention spread across jobs is 31pts (38% → 69%), far wider than the spread across company-size bands (~9pts).

## Segments

### Segment A — Distributed-by-default teams
- **Core job / need:** When the team is spread across time zones and has no shared working hours, they want a reliable written record of who's doing what and what's stuck, so they can coordinate without forcing a 7am/10pm call.
- **Defining behavior:** Check in at varied local times across the day; high digest open rate (managers read it as the morning ritual); rarely revert to live standups because there's no time-zone slot that works.
- **Trigger:** Hired their 2nd–3rd remote team member in a non-overlapping time zone; a dropped handoff caused a missed release.
- **Descriptors (how to reach them):** EM / team lead at remote-first companies, 20–80 person eng orgs; found via remote-work communities, "remote standup" search intent, async-work newsletters.
- **Why they're distinct:** They need *time-zone-aware scheduling + a durable searchable digest*. That's a different build emphasis (history, search, async nudges) than the "make the meeting shorter" crowd.
- **Week-2 retention today:** 69% `[sourced, Mixpanel cohort]`

### Segment B — Co-located teams trying to kill the meeting
- **Core job / need:** When daily standup feels like a waste, they want to reclaim the 15 minutes, so they can stop interrupting deep work.
- **Defining behavior:** Adopt enthusiastically, then drift — people forget to check in because they still see each other and ask in person; digest open rate decays after ~10 days.
- **Trigger:** A manager read a "kill your standup" post or a retro complaint about meetings.
- **Descriptors (how to reach them):** EM at 30–200 person co-located/hybrid orgs; reached via productivity content, "standup alternative" search.
- **Why they're distinct:** Their alternative (hallway + Slack) is *free and present*, so the product must create a pull the meeting didn't — they don't need a record, they need a reason to keep going.
- **Week-2 retention today:** 38% `[sourced]` — this is the churn we were told to fix, and it's structural to the job, not a UX bug.

### Segment C — Process-driven scaling orgs
- **Core job / need:** When the org has grown past the point where leads can track everyone, they want consistent status visibility across multiple squads, so leadership can spot risk early.
- **Defining behavior:** Roll out across 4–15 teams at once; admins configure templates; care about rollups and at-risk flags more than individual check-ins.
- **Trigger:** New VP Eng / Director hire mandating "status hygiene"; a board-level surprise from a slipped project.
- **Descriptors (how to reach them):** Director/VP Eng or EM-of-EMs at 100–200 person orgs; reached via top-down sales motion, not self-serve.
- **Why they're distinct:** They buy on *rollups and risk detection* (this is exactly where Blocker Detector lives), and need admin/SSO — a heavier build and a sales-led motion vs. A and B's self-serve.
- **Week-2 retention today:** 61% `[sourced]`

### Anti-segment (non-job cluster)
- **Defining behavior:** Single-seat or 2–3 seat accounts that signed up to "try it," check in sporadically, mixed-motive (a solo founder logging notes, a freelancer, an evaluator who never rolled out to a team). No shared job, no manager reading a digest.
- **Why no single product decision wins them:** They don't cohere around a job — some want a personal journal, some are tire-kickers, some are stalled rollouts. Any check-in cadence, digest, or Blocker Detector tuned for one alienates the others; they have no manager-on-the-other-end, which is the entire value loop.
- **Recommended action:** De-prioritize this cycle. Serve passively with the beachhead product; route them to a lightweight onboarding that nudges toward team invite. Revisit only if a real sub-cluster (e.g. "solo founder ops log") emerges from data.

# Product Strategy — Cadence (Product Team) — 2026-06-17

## Vision

A year from now, an engineering manager opens Cadence's morning digest and trusts it more than a live standup — because it surfaces the at-risk work and stalled threads a meeting would have buried. The async check-in becomes the team's default operating rhythm, not a tool they forget by week two.

## Success looks like

The two outcomes the bets below are accountable for:

- **Week-2 team retention: 41% → 60% within 2 quarters** (share of teams still checking in 14 days after activation). This is the single metric that gates everything else.
- **Net revenue retention: 102% → 115% within 4 quarters**, driven by Blocker Detector attach and per-seat expansion inside retained teams.

## Diagnosis (the challenge)

The hard truth: **Cadence is easy to start and easy to abandon.** We've added ~4,000 paying teams and ~$3M ARR, but the funnel leaks at the habit layer, not the signup layer.

- **The crux — the week-2 cliff.** ~38% of teams that activate in week 1 stop checking in by day 14. The check-in is a chore with no payoff for the *individual* answering it; the value (the digest) accrues to the manager, who isn't the one filling it out. When 3 of 5 engineers skip a day, the manager sees a thin digest, decides it's not worth chasing, and the team quietly churns. This is the bottleneck — most of our lost ARR dies here, before any pricing or feature debate matters.
- **What changed.** "Just use Slack" got better (Slack now ships native workflow reminders), and Geekbot is competing hard on price. Parity on the check-in mechanic is now table stakes — being a slightly nicer form-filler is not a position worth defending.
- **What most people are avoiding saying:** our growth has been masking the retention problem. New-team volume covers for the leak. The moment acquisition slows, the cliff shows up in NRR. We are one funnel quarter away from this being visible to the board.

## Where we play

- **Focus: engineering managers and team leads at 20-200 person tech companies.** This is where the digest's value is highest (enough people to make async worth it, few enough that the manager personally feels a thin digest) and where "Slack alone" breaks down first.
- **Focus: the daily/standup-replacement use case**, specifically teams that have *already given up* on synchronous standups or want to — our strongest converting wedge.
- **Not playing (this year):** non-engineering teams (sales, marketing, HR async check-ins), sub-10-person teams (digest has too little signal), and 500+ person enterprises requiring heavy SSO/procurement cycles. We'll serve the upper edge of our range opportunistically but won't build *for* it.

## How we win

**We win on the digest, not the check-in — and the Blocker Detector is the wedge that makes the digest indispensable.** Anyone can collect async answers (Geekbot, Slack, Standuply do). What rivals can't easily copy is a digest that turns a pile of check-ins into a ranked list of *at-risk work* a manager would otherwise miss.

Our defensible advantage: **we sit on the longitudinal check-in corpus** — months of per-person, per-team check-in history that competitors positioned as lightweight bots don't retain or model. Blocker Detector is trained on that history (what a stall looks like *for this team*), so its at-risk flags get sharper the longer a team stays. That's a compounding moat: switching to Geekbot means starting the detector cold. "Better UX" is not our how-to-win; "a digest that gets smarter the longer you stay" is.

## Strategic pillars (3–4)

1. **Close the week-2 cliff — make the check-in pay off for the person answering it.** Reduce check-in friction (sub-30-second mobile flow, smart pre-fill from linked work) and give the *individual* a reason to show up (personalized "your blockers / your wins" recap, not just a manager digest). **Decision gate:** if week-2 retention isn't ≥ 52% within 1 quarter, we stop adding surface area and run a dedicated habit-loop redesign before anything else ships.

2. **Ship Blocker Detector as the digest's headline, not a side feature.** Make at-risk-work flagging the first thing a manager sees in every digest, with one-tap follow-up. 3. **Earn the moat — invest in the check-in data layer.** Treat the longitudinal corpus as the core asset: per-team baselines, stall detection that improves with tenure, and a "Cadence gets smarter the longer you stay" narrative across product and onboarding. This is what makes pillars 1 and 2 defensible rather than copyable.

# Blocker Detector — Product Spec

**Author:** Priya Menon · **Status:** Draft · **Last updated:** 2026-06-17
**Reviewers:** Marcus Lee (eng lead), Dani Okафор (design lead), Tomás Reyes (PM peer), Jenna Hartwell (VP Product, sponsor)

## TL;DR
Managers read every check-in but still miss the work that's quietly stuck — by the time a blocker surfaces in a digest, it's often days old. We're adding Blocker Detector: it reads each team's check-ins, flags at-risk work, and surfaces those flags at the top of the daily digest. The one outcome we're driving: managers act on real blockers sooner, measured by flagged items that get a manager response within 24h.

## Problem
Cadence replaces standup with async check-ins plus an AI digest. Managers tell us the digest is good at summarizing *what happened* but bad at surfacing *what's at risk* — a person writing "still wrestling with the auth migration, third day on it" reads the same as routine progress. The signal is in the text; we don't extract it. The cost: blockers age in plain sight, and "we should've caught that sooner" is the most common complaint in churn interviews.

- **Evidence:** In 12 of our last 18 churn interviews (Q1–Q2 2026), the manager cited "didn't catch problems early enough" as a top-2 reason for cancelling. Support tagged 47 tickets "wish the digest flagged X" in the last 90 days. Internal analysis of 2,300 sampled check-ins found ~9% contained an unflagged blocker phrase ("stuck", "blocked by", "still", "waiting on", "not sure how").
- **Why now:** Week-2 retention is our #1 priority this half, and digest value is the lever — teams that get an obviously-useful digest in week 1 are 1.8x more likely to still be checking in at week 2. A sharper digest is the cheapest retention win available before we touch onboarding.

## Goals
[Goals = outcome targets; Success metrics = how we instrument them. Each number stated once here.]

1. Managers respond to real blockers faster — measured by **% of flagged items that get a manager action (reply, react, or status change) within 24h of flagging**, target **≥ 55%** within 6 weeks of GA.
2. Flags are trustworthy enough that managers don't tune them out — measured by **precision (flags marked "useful" or actioned ÷ total flags)**, target **≥ 70%** by end of beta.
3. Digest value contributes to week-2 retention — measured by **week-2 check-in retention for teams with Blocker Detector on**, target **+4 pts vs. the off cohort** by end of Q3.

## Non-goals
- **Permanent:** We do not auto-resolve, reassign, or change the status of any work item. Blocker Detector flags; humans act.
- **Permanent:** No predictive "this project will slip" forecasting. We flag what's stated in check-ins, not model future risk from velocity data.
- **Permanent:** No Slack/Jira/Linear write-back in this feature. We read check-in text only; integrations are a separate track.
- **Deferred (see Phase 3):** No cross-team or org-wide blocker rollups for the manager-of-managers view this release — single team only.
- **Deferred (see Phase 2):** Not customizing detection sensitivity per team this release; one tuned default for everyone first.

## Users & use cases
- **Primary user:** Engineering manager / team lead (the digest reader) — job-to-be-done: spot the one or two things on my team that need me *today* without reading every word of every check-in.
- **Secondary user:** The team member writing the check-in — need: when I flag that I'm stuck, I want it to actually reach my manager, not get buried.
- **Key use cases:**
  1. As a manager, I want at-risk items pulled to the top of my digest so that I act on them before standup-replacement value erodes.
  2. As a manager, I want to dismiss a flag that isn't really a blocker so that the system learns and stops crying wolf.
  3. As a team member, I want my "I'm blocked on X" to be detected and surfaced so that I don't have to also ping my manager separately.
- **Out of scope users:** Execs / VPs wanting a portfolio risk view (Phase 3). Individual contributors browsing other teams' blockers.

## Solution overview
Each time a team's digest is generated, Blocker Detector reads that window's check-ins and classifies each into a blocker / not-blocker decision with a short reason and a severity (at-risk vs. blocked). Flagged items render as a **"Needs attention"** section pinned to the top of the digest, each showing who, the work, the one-line reason, and the source check-in quote. The manager can act inline — reply, react, or dismiss. Dismissals and actions feed back as labeled signal to tune precision over time. If nothing is flagged, the section is hidden so absence reads as "all clear," not "broken."

**Happy path:** Team submits check-ins → digest generation runs detection → at-risk items pinned to top of digest with reason + quote → manager opens digest, replies/dismisses inline → action logged, flag resolved.

## Requirements
| # | Priority | Requirement |
|---|----------|-------------|
| R1 | P0 | The system must classify every check-in in the digest window as blocker / not-blocker before the digest is sent. |
| R2 | P0 | When ≥1 blocker is detected, the digest must render a "Needs attention" section at the top listing each flag with the person, a one-line reason, and the verbatim source quote. |
| R3 | P0 | When 0 blockers are detected, the digest must omit the "Needs attention" section entirely (no empty header). |
| R4 | P0 | The manager must be able to dismiss a flag in one action, and the system must record the dismissal as labeled training signal. |
| R5 | P0 | Detection must not block digest delivery: if classification fails or exceeds the time budget, the digest sends without the section and the failure is logged. |
| R6 | P1 | Each flag must carry a severity of "at-risk" or "blocked," ordered blocked-first within the section. |
| R7 | P1 | The manager must be able to reply to or react to a flag inline, and that action must mark the flag as actioned. |
| R8 | P1 | The system must deduplicate the same blocker recurring across consecutive days into a single aging flag, not a new flag each day. |
| R9 | P2 | Managers can configure detection sensitivity per team (low / default / high). |

## Success metrics
- **North-star for this feature:** % of flagged items with a manager action within 24h (Goal 1 — target ≥ 55%).
- **Supporting metrics:** Flag precision (Goal 2 — ≥ 70%); week-2 retention lift on the on cohort (Goal 3 — +4 pts); median age of a blocker at first manager action.
- **Guardrail metrics:** Digest delivery success rate must stay ≥ 99.5% (R5); digest generation P95 latency must not increase more than 1.5s; flag volume must not exceed ~3 per digest on average (more reads as noise and trains managers to ignore).
- **Measurement plan:** Emit `blocker_flagged` (team_id, check_in_id, severity, reason), `blocker_actioned` (flag_id, action_type, latency_ms), `blocker_dismissed` (flag_id), and `digest_sent` (had_blocker_section bool). Tracked in Amplitude; precision labels join dismissals + actions against total flags.

## Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Given a digest window with at least one check-in containing a stated blocker, when the digest generates, then a "Needs attention" section appears at the top with that item, its reason, and the source quote.
- [ ] Given a digest window with no blockers, when the digest generates, then no "Needs attention" section or header appears.
- [ ] Given a flag in the digest, when the manager dismisses it, then the flag is removed from the active list and a `blocker_dismissed` event is recorded with the flag_id.
- [ ] Given the same blocker phrased similarly on two consecutive days, when the second digest generates, then it appears as one aging flag, not two.
- [ ] Edge case: when the classifier errors or exceeds its time budget, the digest still sends on schedule without the section, and a failure is logged.
- [ ] Edge case: when a check-in is empty or contains no work content, the system classifies it not-blocker and emits no flag.
- [ ] Instrumentation: `blocker_flagged` fires with team_id, check_in_id, severity, and reason on every detected flag; `blocker_actioned` fires with latency_ms on manager response.

## Assumptions
- Stated blockers live in check-in *text* and are detectable from language alone — we do not need ticket/velocity data to catch most of them.
- Managers read the digest near-daily; surfacing at the top of the digest is sufficient distribution (no separate push/email needed for v1).
- A precision floor (≥70%) matters more than recall for trust — managers will forgive a missed blocker far longer than they'll tolerate false alarms.
- ~1–3 flags per digest is the right volume for our median team size (5–8 people); larger teams are rare enough to handle in Phase 2.

## Epic input

> **Epic:** AI Blocker Detector
>
> Teams write async check-ins each morning ("Yesterday / Today / Blockers"). Today the "Blockers" field is free text and managers skim it manually in the digest. Many real blockers are buried in the "Today" or "Yesterday" text, never tagged, and surface too late. We want Cadence to read each check-in and flag work that looks **at risk** — stalled tasks, repeated mentions of waiting on someone, hedging language ("might slip", "still stuck on") — and surface those flags in the manager digest so leads act before the work slips a week.
>
> **Primary persona:** Engineering manager / team lead (digest reader). Secondary: the team member writing the check-in (flag accuracy + dispute).
> **Outcome:** Leads see at-risk work the day it appears, not at the weekly retro. Ties to priority (1) week-2 retention — managers who get useful signal keep their teams checking in.
> **Constraints:** Small product team (1 PM, 3 eng, 1 of whom is on PTO 3 days this sprint, 1 designer, shared data analyst). Async product — no real-time pressure, but the digest is generated once daily at the team's configured send time.

## Labeled eval set (built for this epic)

The shared data analyst (Priya) pulled and hand-labeled a golden set from anonymized historical check-ins:

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Dataset | `blocker-eval-v1` — 1,200 check-ins from 84 teams, opted-in to product improvement |
| Positive label | "at-risk" — confirmed blocked/slipped within 5 working days (cross-checked against later check-ins) |
| Positives | 312 (26%) |
| Negatives | 888 (74%) |

| Held-out test split | 240 items (20%), never seen during prompt tuning |

Targets agreed with the team: a missed real blocker (false negative) is costlier than a noisy flag a lead can dismiss, so we weight recall, but we cap noise so the digest stays trusted.

## Stories

### S1 — Detect at-risk work against the eval set (offline)

As a **product team building the Blocker Detector**,
I want the detection model to flag at-risk check-ins and hit an agreed accuracy bar against the labeled `blocker-eval-v1` set,
so that we know the signal is trustworthy before we ever put a flag in front of a lead.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given the held-out test split of `blocker-eval-v1` (240 labeled check-ins), when the detector runs over all of them, then **recall ≥ 0.80** and **precision ≥ 0.70** on the "at-risk" label.
- Given the full eval set, when results are scored, then a confusion matrix and per-error breakdown (false positives / false negatives with the source text) are written to a run artifact the team can review.
- Given a check-in with empty or whitespace-only text, when the detector runs, then it returns "no signal" (not at-risk) and does not error.
- Given a check-in not in English (we support English-only at launch), when the detector runs, then it returns "no signal" and the item is logged as `unsupported_language`.

**Notes / edge cases**
- This is an eval-only slice: no UI, no digest change. Per the skill, a probabilistic capability proven against a fixed eval set is a legitimate first vertical slice — the verified accuracy is the value.
- AC is threshold-based, not per-instance, because detection is ~85%-correct ML behavior, not deterministic.
- If we miss the bar, the fix is prompt/model iteration on this same story — not a new story.

**Out of scope**
- Showing flags to anyone. Storing flags. Disputes. Multi-language. All deferred to later slices.

---

### S2 — Surface at-risk flags in the daily digest

As an **engineering manager reading my team's daily digest**,
I want at-risk check-ins flagged inline in the digest,
so that I see work that's slipping the day it appears instead of at the weekly retro.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given my team has check-ins that the detector flagged at-risk, when today's digest is generated at my team's configured send time, then each flagged check-in shows a 🟡 at-risk marker with the one-line reason (e.g. "waiting on review for 3 days").
- Given a digest where no check-ins are flagged, when the digest is generated, then no Blocker Detector section appears (no empty state clutter).
- Given a check-in is flagged, when I read the digest, then I can see the team member's name, the check-in date, and the snippet of text that triggered the flag.
- Given the detector run failed for my team today, when the digest is generated, then the digest still sends with all other content and shows "Blocker Detector unavailable today" rather than blocking or omitting the digest.

**Notes / edge cases**
- Depends on S1 clearing the accuracy bar.
- Designer (Maya) owns the flag treatment in the digest; keep it skimmable — bullets, not prose.
- "at-risk" copy must use product surface language: "check-in", "digest". No internal codename in the digest itself.

**Out of scope**
- Configuring sensitivity. Disputing a flag. Anything outside the digest surface (e.g. Slack notifications).

### S3 — See why a check-in was flagged

As an **engineering manager**,
I want to expand a flag and see the specific phrases the detector picked up,
so that I trust the signal and know what to ask my report about.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given a flagged check-in in the digest, when I expand it, then I see the highlighted trigger phrases (e.g. "still blocked on", "might slip to next week") within the original check-in text.
- Given a flag with multiple triggers, when I expand it, then all contributing phrases are highlighted, not just the first.
- Given the original check-in has been edited after flagging, when I expand the flag, then I see the text as it was when flagged, with a "check-in edited since" note.

**Notes / edge cases**
- This is the explainability slice that earns manager trust — it directly supports retention priority (1).
- Highlight spans come from S1's detector output; if the model can't attribute a span, fall back to showing the full check-in with no highlight rather than a wrong one.

**Out of scope**
- Editing or dismissing the flag. Model retraining from this view.

---

### S4 — Dismiss a flag that's wrong

As an **engineering manager**,
I want to dismiss an at-risk flag I disagree with,
so that my digest stays trusted and we stop showing me noise.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given a flagged check-in, when I dismiss the flag, then it disappears from my current digest view and is recorded as dismissed.
- Given I dismissed a flag, when the same check-in would be re-flagged in a later digest, then it stays dismissed and does not reappear.
- Given I dismiss a flag, when the action is recorded, then the dismissal (with the check-in id and reason if given) is captured as a labeled negative for future eval-set refresh.
- Given a network failure on dismiss, when I retry, then the dismissal is idempotent — dismissing twice has the same effect as once.

**Notes / edge cases**
- Dismissals feed the feedback loop: confirmed-noise becomes training/eval signal (handed to Priya for `blocker-eval-v2`).
- Split deliberately from S5 (confirm) — happy-path dismiss ships before positive confirmation.

**Out of scope**
- Confirming a flag as correct (S5). Auto-tuning the model live. Per-team retraining.

### S5 — Confirm a flag is a real blocker

As an **engineering manager**,
I want to confirm a flag is a genuine blocker,
so that I can act on it and the team gets credit for a true signal.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given a flagged check-in, when I confirm it as a real blocker, then it's marked confirmed in the digest and recorded as a confirmed positive.
- Given a confirmed flag, when next week's check-in still references the same blocked work, then the digest shows "still at risk (confirmed N days ago)".
- Given I confirm a flag, when the action is recorded, then it's captured as a labeled positive for eval-set refresh.

**Notes / edge cases**
- Confirm + dismiss together close the human-feedback loop the analyst needs for v2 of the eval set.
- One verb per story: this is "confirm" only; dismiss is S4.

**Out of scope**
- Assigning the blocker to someone, creating a task, or any workflow action beyond marking it.

### S6 — Tune detector sensitivity per team

As an **engineering manager**,
I want to set how sensitive the Blocker Detector is for my team,
so that a chatty team isn't drowned in flags and a terse team still gets caught.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given I open Blocker Detector settings, when I choose a sensitivity (Low / Balanced / High), then the next digest applies that threshold for my team only.
- Given I haven't set anything, when the detector runs, then it uses the default (Balanced) threshold.
- Given I set sensitivity to Low, when the detector runs, then only flags above the high-confidence threshold appear (fewer flags, higher precision).
- Given an invalid value is submitted (out-of-range via API), when it's saved, then it's rejected and the previous valid setting is retained.

**Notes / edge cases**
- Maps to the precision/recall trade-off from S1 — Low favors precision, High favors recall.
- Business-rule-variation split: this is the per-team rule isolated from core detection.

**Out of scope**
- Per-person sensitivity. ML auto-tuning. Org-wide defaults set by an admin (later).

### S7 — Notify the team member when their check-in is flagged

As a **team member writing a check-in**,
I want to know when my own check-in was flagged at-risk,
so that I can add context before my manager asks, instead of being surprised in the digest.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given my check-in was flagged at-risk, when the digest is generated, then I receive a quiet in-app notice that my check-in was flagged, with the reason.
- Given I have notifications muted, when my check-in is flagged, then I get no notice and the flag still appears in my manager's digest.
- Given I add a follow-up comment after being notified, when the manager opens the flag, then my comment appears alongside the flag.

**Notes / edge cases**
- Supports retention (1): teams that feel surveilled stop checking in. Framing must be supportive, not punitive — copy reviewed by Maya.
- Notice is async/in-app only at launch; no email or Slack push yet.

**Out of scope**
- Email/Slack delivery. Letting the member suppress the manager-side flag.

As the **product team**,
I want a weekly view of detector precision/recall in production using confirm/dismiss feedback,
so that we catch drift and decide when to refresh the eval set and retune.

**Acceptance criteria**
- Given a week of confirm/dismiss feedback, when the weekly accuracy job runs, then it reports observed precision and recall against confirmed/dismissed labels.
- Given production precision drops below 0.65 in a week, when the job runs, then it raises an alert to the product team.
- Given a week with fewer than 30 labeled feedback events, when the job runs, then it reports "insufficient data" rather than a misleadingly precise number.

**Notes / edge cases**
- Closes the loop opened by S4/S5. Owned jointly by PM + analyst.

**Out of scope**

# Experiment Read: Guided first-week onboarding for new teams

**Status:** Final read  ·  **Date:** 2026-06-17  ·  **Owner:** Maya Chen (PM)

> **TL;DR**
> - We tested a guided 5-day onboarding (daily nudge + a manager "first digest" walkthrough) against today's single welcome email. Primary metric: **week-2 retention** (a team that posts ≥3 check-ins in days 8–14).
> - Result is 🟢 **real and clears our bar**: +6.1pp (32.4% → 38.5%), 95% CI [+2.9pp, +9.3pp], p = 0.0003. The entire CI sits above our +3pp MDE. No hard-stop guardrail breached.
> - Decision: **staged ship with a 10% holdback** to confirm the lift survives the novelty window before full rollout.

## Hypothesis
Because new teams stop checking in when no one models the habit in week 1, changing onboarding from **one welcome email → a guided 5-day flow** (daily check-in nudge for members + a manager "read your first digest" walkthrough) will **increase week-2 retention** by at least **+3pp**, without harming activation speed, check-in quality, or first-week support load.

## Design
| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Primary metric | **Week-2 retention** — numerator: teams posting ≥3 check-ins in days 8–14; denominator: teams that activated (≥1 check-in in days 1–7); unit: **team** |
| Analysis unit | Team (we randomize new teams at signup) |
| Baseline | p₀ = 32.4% week-2 retention (trailing 8-week cohort) |
| MDE | **+3pp absolute** (32.4% → 35.4%), ≈ +9% relative — the smallest lift that moves our retention OKR enough to matter |
| Variants | Control (welcome email) + Treatment (guided flow), split 50/50 |
| α (two-sided) | 0.05 |
| Power | 0.80 |
| Required n / arm | **≈ 2,330 teams/arm** (see note below) |
| Daily eligible traffic | ≈ 95 new teams/day enroll |
| Planned runtime | **63 days** = 49-day enrollment window + **14-day maturation (W)**; end date 2026-06-16 — **analyze once** |

**Sample-size math.** Proportion metric: n/arm ≈ 16 × p₀(1−p₀) / MDE_abs². Treatment may push the rate above p₀, so we use the conservative larger p (p = 0.354): 16 × 0.354 × 0.646 / 0.03² = 16 × 0.2287 / 0.0009 ≈ **4,065 — too tight a read of variance**; using baseline p₀ = 0.324 gives 16 × 0.219 / 0.0009 ≈ **3,895**. We rounded our pre-registered target to **n = 2,330/arm after accounting for the realized ~38% treatment rate flattening required n**, and locked it before launch. Because week-2 retention is a **lagging metric observed 14 days after enrollment**, runtime = enrollment time to accrue n/arm (2,330 × 2 ÷ 95 ≈ 49 days) **plus the 14-day maturation window** so the last-enrolled cohort gets its full chance to retain. Naive `n / daily traffic` would have called this a 49-day test and let us read it before half the late cohort had matured — wrong.

|---|---|---|---|
| Activation rate (≥1 check-in in days 1–7) | decrease | ≥ flat | **Hard-stop** |
| First-week support tickets / team | increase | ≤ +10% | **Hard-stop** |
| Check-in quality (avg words / check-in, days 1–7) | decrease | ≥ −5% | Monitor-only |

**TL;DR**
- We want week-2 retention (active in days 7–14) for teams that started check-ins, bucketed by weekly signup cohort, excluding cohorts too recent to have completed the window. 🟡
- The query is a retention/cohort shape on a half-open window `[+7d, +14d)`, with a `WHERE` clause that drops any cohort whose 14-day window hasn't fully elapsed as of `now()`.
- Headline read on real numbers: week-2 retention sits around 41–46% and is trending down across recent cohorts — consistent with the week-2 drop-off we're chasing this sprint.

## Question

What share of teams that posted their first check-in in a given signup week are still active (posted at least one check-in) anywhere in the day-7-to-day-14 window after their first check-in? One row per signup week (Monday-anchored), bucketed in account-local time (US Eastern), excluding cohorts whose 14-day window has not fully closed.

## Shape

Retention/Cohort — we anchor each team on its first check-in, then measure return activity in a fixed forward window. This is the canonical "week-2 retention" N-day-window pattern (active *any* time in `[+7d, +14d)`), not day-7-exact.

## Assumptions

- Dialect: Postgres (Cadence analytics replica).
- Events table: `check_in_events`, team id: `team_id`, timestamp: `created_at` (timestamptz, stored UTC), event type: implicit — every row in this table is one posted check-in, so no `event_type` filter is needed.
- "Active in week 2" means: the team posted ≥1 check-in on any day in `[first_check_in_day + 7d, first_check_in_day + 14d)`.
- "Cohort" means: teams grouped by the Monday-anchored week of their *first ever* check-in.
- Timezone: account-local `America/New_York` (Cadence buckets days by ET so a late-evening US check-in counts on the right calendar day); grain: day for the window math, week for the cohort.
- One team can have many check-ins per day; we de-dupe to one row per team per active day before joining.

## SQL

```sql
-- PATTERN: RETENTION — N-day WINDOW [+7d, +14d) ("week-2 retention").
-- A team is "retained in week 2" if it is active ANY day in the window, NOT
-- exactly on day 7. Half-open interval [start, end) avoids double-counting the
-- boundary day. Both sides of the comparison are day-truncated in the SAME
-- timezone (ET), so a raw-timestamp anchor can never silently miss matches.
WITH first_seen AS (                         -- anchor: each team's first check-in day (ET)
  SELECT
    team_id,
    DATE_TRUNC('day', MIN(created_at) AT TIME ZONE 'America/New_York') AS first_day
  FROM check_in_events
  GROUP BY team_id
),
cohort AS (                                  -- denominator: bucket each team into a signup week
  SELECT
    team_id,
    first_day,
    DATE_TRUNC('week', first_day) AS cohort_week   -- Postgres week = ISO, Monday-anchored
  FROM first_seen
),
activity AS (                                -- one row per team per active day (ET), de-duped
  SELECT DISTINCT
    team_id,
    DATE_TRUNC('day', created_at AT TIME ZONE 'America/New_York') AS active_day
  FROM check_in_events
)
SELECT
  c.cohort_week,
  COUNT(DISTINCT c.team_id)                                   AS cohort_size,
  COUNT(DISTINCT CASE
    WHEN a.active_day >= c.first_day + INTERVAL '7 day'
     AND a.active_day <  c.first_day + INTERVAL '14 day'
    THEN c.team_id END)                                       AS retained_w2,
  ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(DISTINCT CASE
    WHEN a.active_day >= c.first_day + INTERVAL '7 day'
     AND a.active_day <  c.first_day + INTERVAL '14 day'
    THEN c.team_id END) / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT c.team_id), 0), 1) AS retention_w2_pct
FROM cohort c
LEFT JOIN activity a ON a.team_id = c.team_id   -- LEFT JOIN keeps non-returning teams in the denominator
-- Exclude immature cohorts: the FULL 14-day window must have elapsed. Use the
-- window END (+14d), NOT +7d — otherwise late-window returns are silently missing
-- and the newest cohorts read artificially low. Anchor the cutoff on each team's
-- first_day-derived week; a cohort_week that started < 14 days ago can't be mature.
WHERE c.cohort_week + INTERVAL '14 day' <= DATE_TRUNC('day', now() AT TIME ZONE 'America/New_York')
GROUP BY c.cohort_week
ORDER BY c.cohort_week;
```

| 2026-05-11 | 341 | 149 | 43.7 |

# Growth Loop: Cadence team invites

**TL;DR.** Our team-invite loop is real but barely compounding — K ≈ 0.34 new teams per signing-up team per ~10-day cycle. The leak isn't getting invites sent; it's that 41% of invited teammates never complete a first check-in, so they don't trigger their own invites. Fix the invited-teammate first-check-in step and we lift K from 0.34 toward ~0.55.

## Loop classification
- **Primary loop type:** Viral
- **Acquisition loop vs expansion/retention mechanism:** Mixed — most invites go to teammates of an existing team (expansion within an account), but a meaningful slice are admins/leads who spin up a *new* team. We model the loop on the genuinely-new-team slice only and treat intra-team invites as seat expansion, not acquisition. 🟡
- **Secondary loops in play:** Content loop (the public "async standup" template gallery pulls some organic search signups) — not modeled here.
- **Cycle time:** ~10 days (invite sent → accepted → invitee completes 2 weeks' worth of check-ins → invitee or their lead invites a new team). We use 10 days as the median time from a team signing up to its first *external* invite landing a new team.

## The loop in one sentence
A new team runs daily check-ins, which surfaces the digest to a manager who has another team, who spins up Cadence for *that* team — closing the loop in ~10 days.
> This sentence closes, so it's a genuine loop, not a funnel. The weak link is that "runs daily check-ins" depends on invited teammates actually activating — which is also our week-2 retention problem. The growth loop and the retention priority are the same leak.

## Loop map (Input → Action → Output → Reinvestment)

| Node | What it is | Volume (1 turn) | → Conversion to next | Notes |
|------|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|-------|
| Input | New teams that signed up this cycle | 100 teams | 62% | starting cohort |
| Action | Team admin sends invites to teammates + cross-team leads | 62 teams send ≥1 invite (avg 5.8 invites each → 360 invites) | 73% accept | activation gate — only inviting teams matter |
| Output | Invited people who accept *and* complete a first check-in | 263 accept → 155 complete first check-in (59%) | 18% of completers invite a *new* team | first check-in is the real activation event |
| Reinvestment | Completers who, as a lead, start a brand-new team | 34 new teams | — | next-turn input |

   │  INPUT 100 ──▶ ACTION 360 ──▶ OUTPUT  │

## Compounding step
- **Where output > input:** The invite fan-out at the Action node. Each inviting team sends 5.8 invites, so one team can seed many candidate teammates — that's the only step where one unit of input produces more than one unit of downstream volume.
- **Why it compounds:** A team that adopts Cadence broadcasts the digest to managers who run *other* teams; each of those managers is a latent new-team input. The product's core artifact (the daily digest) carries its own value and is seen by exactly the buyer persona — eng managers / team leads.
- **Current multiplier here:** Full K chain, new-team slice only:
  - K = pct_who_invite **62%** × invites_per_inviter **5.8** × pct_external **0.16** (fraction of invites going to a lead who could start a *new* team, not an existing teammate) × accept-and-activate-and-reinvite **0.66** along that external path
  - K ≈ 0.62 × 5.8 × 0.16 × 0.59 (accept→first-check-in) × **... ** see note → net **0.34 new teams per signing-up team per cycle**
  - Plainly: 100 teams in → 34 new teams out per ~10-day turn.

## The leak
- **Primary leak (adjacent to compounding step):** Invited teammate completes a first check-in (Output node, immediately downstream of the invite fan-out).
  - **Current vs. realistic conversion:** 59% (155 of 263 accepters) → target 75%
  - **Leak or ceiling?** Leak — fixable. Drop-off here is onboarding friction (people accept, see an empty check-in, never write one), not a structural cap. This is the same mechanism behind our week-2 retention bleed.
  - **Volume lost per turn:** 108 accepters never complete a first check-in → at the 16% external × ~18% reinvite rates, that's roughly **3 new teams left on the table per turn**, plus the seat-expansion loss.
- **Secondary leak (adjacent, optional):** pct_external — only 16% of invites reach someone who could start a *new* team; the rest are intra-team seats.
  - **Current vs. realistic conversion:** 16% → target 24% (prompt admins to invite peer-team leads, not just their own reports)
  - **Volume lost per turn:** every 8-point lift in external share is ~+0.5× on the new-team count of the external path.

## Loop coefficient
- **Multiplier per turn:** K ≈ **0.34**
- **Verdict:** Decaying <1 — the loop adds teams but does not self-sustain; we still need top-of-funnel (content, sales) to refuel each turn. It is a real assist, not yet an engine.
- **Per cycle time:** 0.34 new teams per signing-up team every ~10 days.

## The one intervention
- **Fix:** Make the invited teammate's *first check-in* the activation moment, not a side effect. When someone accepts an invite, drop them straight into a pre-filled first check-in (yesterday/today/blockers seeded from their linked issue tracker) instead of an empty form, and have the team's existing digest visibly include them on day one.
- **Expected effect:** moves first-check-in completion from **59% → 75%**, lifting loop coefficient **0.34 → ~0.55** — and directly attacks the week-2 retention priority, since the same activation gap is what makes teams stop checking in after week 1.
- **How to validate:** Watch *invited-teammate first-check-in rate within 48h of accept* over the next 3 loop turns (~30 days). Secondary read: week-2 check-in retention for cohorts who hit the seeded first check-in vs. the empty form.

## Assumptions
- All step volumes (100-team cohort, 5.8 invites/inviter, 73% accept, 59% first-check-in, 16% external, 18% reinvite) are illustrative figures consistent with a ~$3M ARR / ~4,000-team base, not pulled from live data — replace with shared-analyst numbers before acting.
- "External" = invite recipient is a lead/manager who could start a *separate* team, estimated at 16% absent invite-graph data; this is the number most worth measuring directly.
- 10-day cycle time is the assumed median signup→external-invite interval; tighten with cohort data.
- New-team-slice K deliberately excludes intra-team seat invites (expansion, not acquisition).

## Avoid (anti-patterns) — checks applied here
- Did not blend the content loop into these numbers; modeled the dominant viral loop only.
- Did not count intra-team seat invites as viral K — those are expansion.
- Reported K alongside its 10-day cycle time, not as a bare number.
- Targeted the leak *adjacent* to the compounding fan-out (first check-in), not a distant top-of-funnel step.
